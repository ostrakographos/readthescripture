On Truth — An Invitation to Read

Some things in a book can be true even when not everything in it is. An essay on fact, framework, story, and Scripture; less an argument than an invitation.

Study
≈12 min read Challenging On reading Foundational
Abstract

This essay separates the question did it happen? from the question is it true? It follows one pattern through gravity, arithmetic, and story, from a schoolyard proverb to Golding's island to the parables, and brings the pattern to the Bible. It argues one narrow claim and closes with an invitation rather than a proof.

Written from an orthodox Christian confession

Introduction

A Smaller Claim Than You Might Expect

Essays that put "truth" and "the Bible" in the same sentence usually reach next for evidence: manuscripts, archaeology, fulfilled prophecy, argument stacked on argument. This one will not. The claim it makes does not need them, and reaching for them would disguise what kind of claim it is.

The claim is this: some things in a book can be true even if not everything in the book is true. That is nearly the whole of what this essay argues. It is a modest claim, and most readers already grant it freely for every book on their shelf except one. The route to it has four steps, and naming them now costs nothing. Truth exists. We understand it only in part. We accept it anyway, because at the ground floor acceptance is not optional. And entering a truth's own framework is how understanding grows. The essay walks those steps and then extends one invitation.

Where I stand belongs on the table before we start. I am a Christian. I believe this book is true in a far stronger sense than the sentence above claims, and the rest of this site says so openly. But that belief is mine, and it is not the argument here. What follows does not attempt a proof and does not want to be one. It is an invitation: to read the book with the same courtesy you extend to every other book that has ever told you something true.

The Ground

Truth Does Not Ask First

Start below argument, at the floor. Nobody negotiates with gravity. You can deny it while dropping a glass, and the glass is unmoved by your position. Almost no one understands it. Physics has described its behavior to exquisite precision, re-described it as the shape of space and time, and still holds two great theories, relativity and quantum mechanics, that have not been made to agree about what gravity finally is. None of that unsettles anything. Your feet accept gravity with every step, understood or not. At the ground floor, truth is like this: it does not wait for a theory, and it does not take a vote.

One floor up, distinctions help. A fact is a verifiable, measurable claim: water boils at 100°C at sea level. Facts can be confirmed or falsified, and they depend on instruments, definitions, and reference points. An observation is what a perceiver records at a moment, real but limited; the same sky looks different to different eyes. An opinion is a judgment: this painting is beautiful, that decision was unwise. Opinions are not facts. But notice the curious case, the proverb. "Cheaters never win and winners never cheat" is an opinion wearing a fact's clothing, and as a fact it fails on any scoreboard you care to check: cheaters win constantly, and winners have cheated. Yet the saying refuses to die, because it was never really counting outcomes. It carries a conviction about what winning is: that a prize taken by fraud is not the thing the game existed to give. Something in that rings true even to the cheater holding the trophy. A sentence can fail as fact and still hold something real. Keep that pattern in view; this essay will meet it again at full length.

Even the largest truth we hold, that the Universe exists, we hold without being able to describe most of what it contains. Dark energy accounts for roughly 68% of its total energy content, dark matter for another 27%, and the visible, star-filled cosmos we actually know amounts to about 5% of the whole. How much does the Universe weigh? A fair question with no answer: weight needs gravity, gravity needs a reference point, and the Universe has no exterior to be weighed from. Time bends the same way, running slower near massive objects, a feature of the thing itself rather than a fixed backdrop behind it. The certainty that the Universe is does not wait on an account of what it weighs or what most of it is made of. We hold truths, comfortably and every day, that we cannot fully describe.

A Working Sense of Truth

Truth, as used here, exists independently of whether anyone has stated it, measured it, witnessed it, or agreed to it. Consensus does not define it, and observation does not generate it. But the one word covers three different kinds of claim, and this essay keeps them apart. The first is existence: the Universe is, described or not. The second is fact: statements that match the world and can be checked against it. The third is the evaluative kind: that justice is a real standard, that love is more than a preference with chemistry underneath. The three are not equally secure. The first is as near undeniable as anything we hold; the second is verified daily; the third is contested ground, with serious company on both sides.

This essay proceeds on the working sense that all three are real: that the injustice of an innocent child's punishment exists whether or not anyone records it. That is an assertion, not a demonstration, and the Criticisms section holds the objection. What no side disputes is that people in every age and culture see these things, and stake their lives on the seeing.

The essay's own examples sort by these senses, and the sorting stays in the open: gravity and the Universe belong to the first; arithmetic's provable truths to the second; what fiction carries, and what this essay will ask you to look for in the Bible, to the third.

The Word Underneath "True"

English "true" leans toward accuracy: a true sentence is one that matches the facts. The word underneath the Hebrew Bible's talk of truth is wider than that. Truth (אֶמֶת | ʾĕmet | Heb - H571) comes from the root ʾāman (H539), to support, to make firm; the standard academic lexicon (Brown-Driver-Briggs) glosses it "firmness, faithfulness, truth." The word does ordinary accuracy work when accuracy is the point: the queen of Sheba tells Solomon "the report was true that I heard in my own land" (1 Kgs 10:6, NRSVue). And usage, not only etymology, carries the weight-bearing sense: in the Bible's oldest self-description of God, ʾĕmet is the word English renders "faithfulness" — "abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness" (Exod 34:6, NRSVue) — the same word elsewhere rendered "truth." ʾĕmûnāh is "faithfulness"; āmēn is "truly"; and the verb heʾĕmîn, to believe, is literally to treat as firm, to lean your weight on, as when Abram "believed the LORD" (Gen 15:6) by resting on a promise. In this vocabulary a true word is not only a word that matches; it is a word that holds. So when the Psalmist says "the sum of your word is truth" (Ps 119:160, NRSVue), the claim is about reliability as much as inventory. Trust, in this book's own language, is what you do about a truth: you put weight on it.

Frameworks

You Must Accept the Framework Before the Truth Within It

Truths are understood within frameworks, and the framework has to be accepted before the truths inside it come within reach. That is not a weakness of human knowledge; it is the structure of human knowledge, and mathematics shows the structure more plainly than anywhere else.

Start with a truth that does not feel true. Most people's first reaction to 0.999… = 1 is that it cannot be right: surely the endless string of nines creeps toward 1 forever and falls just short forever. But inside standard arithmetic, where an infinite decimal means the limit of the sequence it names, the two are not approximately equal. They are the same number, exactly and necessarily. No amount of standing outside arithmetic resolves the discomfort, because the discomfort never came from the math; it came from the standing outside. Notice what was never required: you do not have to like the rule about infinite decimals, then or ever. Acceptance is not agreement. It is the price of admission, and it is paid before understanding, not after.

Work It Yourself

Let x = 0.999… Multiply both sides by ten: 10x = 9.999… Subtract the first line from the second, and the nines cancel digit for digit: 9x = 9. So x = 1. This is the classroom demonstration, not the foundations; it leans on facts about infinite decimals that a course in analysis proves properly. But following it is enough to feel this section's point from the inside: every step obeys rules you already accept, the conclusion arrives, and the discomfort survives its own proof. What dissolves the discomfort is not more argument. It is working inside the system until the definition becomes yours.

There is a deeper fact about that admission price: inside mathematics it is never refunded. In 1931 Kurt Gödel proved that any consistent formal system rich enough to do ordinary arithmetic contains true statements it cannot prove, and that among the statements it cannot prove is its own consistency. To certify that arithmetic will never contradict itself, you must stand in a stronger system, which cannot certify itself either, and so on upward without a top. Every working mathematician therefore operates inside a framework whose own soundness is trusted without proof, permanently. The most rigorous framework human beings have ever built runs on a trust it cannot generate for itself, and it works gloriously anyway.

The Same Posture in Physics

Physicists working inside quantum mechanics sometimes call their posture "shut up and calculate": accept the mathematical framework and use it productively, even though no one fully agrees on what it means at the level of physical reality. The framework earns its trust not by being proven from outside but by producing coherent, reliable results inside. That trust is not proof, and it is not irrational: it is the working scientist's daily form of acceptance before understanding.

Mathematics is only where the pattern is easiest to see. The truths of the psyche were resisted as insults before they were accepted as descriptions: that grief does its work on its own schedule and will not be argued out of it; that a mind can hide its own motives from itself. Conscience speaks before it can explain itself. And the moral territory behaves the same way: cruelty is wrong and kindness is good, seen by people who share no theory and could not defend one, while every system built to codify the seeing remains disputed. Gravity again, one floor up: the pull is undeniable, and the theory of it is unfinished. The things that are true are not always intuitive, and the deepest of them are accepted long before they are understood.

Said once, plainly: none of this makes the Bible more likely to be true, and none of it is on the page to do that. The arithmetic is here to describe an act, the act of reading from inside a system you have provisionally accepted; it is not here to lend anything its authority. What the description removes is exactly one excuse: the idea that accepting-before-understanding is a peculiar demand religion makes and rigorous people refuse. It is how every framework is entered, including the most rigorous one there is. The kinds of acceptance differ, and the difference stays in the open: gravity's cannot be withheld, arithmetic's is collected by proof, and the acceptance this essay will ask for is the weakest kind, the kind a reader is free to decline — which is why what follows can only be an invitation. And entering a framework changes your access to its truths, not their status as truths. What stays outside when you stay outside is not the truth; it is your reach.

An Author's Comment — Opinion, Not Description

Here is the nearest picture I have, offered as my own proposal and nothing more: the Bible is some kind of formula. I mean that in the sense this section has been developing, a text with internal rules (genre, covenant, idiom, a truth-word that means what holds) that yields its meaning only to a reader who provisionally accepts those rules while reading, much as arithmetic does. You do not have to understand all of mathematics to learn from it, and you do not have to agree with every rule; but for it to work, you have to accept it. I think the same is true of this book. "Some kind of" is not decoration: a formula is a picture, not a definition, and some believers will find the picture too cold while some skeptics will find it too generous. I keep it because it names what I am asking of a reader: not belief, not agreement, just acceptance long enough to read. And the picture marks its own limit. Arithmetic, once entered, compels its conclusions. This book, on this essay's terms, only invites.

Story

What Fiction Proves

In 1954 William Golding published Lord of the Flies. None of it happened. There was no island, no stranded schoolboys, no conch, no signal fire, no Piggy. By the accuracy standard, its story reports nothing that happened. And yet readers have come away from it for seventy years knowing something true: that the order we live inside is thinner than it looks, that cruelty does not need to be taught, that the beast the boys hunt in the forest is not out in the trees. No reader withholds that recognition until the island's coordinates are produced. The two questions, did it happen and is it true, come apart the moment you hold them side by side: the first asks about fact, the second, as Golding's readers mean it, about the evaluative kind. Nobody actually reads as though they were one question.

The pattern repeats wherever stories do their deepest work. To Kill a Mockingbird carries a truth about justice without arguing it: Atticus Finch does everything right and loses, the jury convicts a man it knows is innocent, and the story offers no consoling ending. It simply makes the injustice specific and felt, and in doing so it appeals to something in the reader that already knows what justice requires even when justice is not delivered. In Charlotte's Web, Charlotte saves Wilbur at the cost of her own strength and dies alone at the fair, having asked for nothing in return; children and adults alike recognize in her what love is when it is fully expressed. In the film Arrival, Louise Banks learns her daughter will die young and chooses to have her anyway: knowing the ending in advance does not change whether the story is worth having.

These recognitions resist explanation more stubbornly than mathematical truth does. We cannot write an equation for love or run a repeatable experiment on belonging. Yet the recognition has been consistent across the readerships these books have reached. We cannot fully say why these things are true, and we recognize them anyway. Fiction, where nothing happened at all, turns out to be a reliable carrier of truth.

Novelists did not discover this pattern. The Bible's own shelf has practiced it from the beginning. Job opens as a framed tale, a man in the land of Uz and a heavenly wager, and spends most of its length as a poetry debate about innocent suffering; readers in every century have met their own anguish in it, and none of that meeting has ever waited on producing Uz on a map. Proverbs collects observations that verify themselves in use. Ecclesiastes still lands after more than two thousand years precisely because it declines to hand the reader a system. And at the center of the Gospels, Jesus teaches by deliberate fiction: the parables. The good Samaritan does not have to have existed for Luke 10 to be true; no listener, then or now, has escaped "Go and do likewise" (Luke 10:37, NRSVue) by asking for the Samaritan's address. Whatever a reader has or has not concluded about the Gospels' history, the parables inside them already work the way Golding works. The book this essay invites you to read has been making this point on its own pages all along.

The Text

The Bible Is Not Lord of the Flies

Here the essay has to be careful in both directions. The Bible is not a novel. It is a library: history and law, poetry and proverb, prophecy, wisdom, letters, apocalypse, composed across a thousand years and more, tethered throughout to real places and real peoples. And it claims what no novel claims: that God has spoken and acted in history. In the working sense's terms, the book makes claims of all three kinds — that God is; that events happened, among real places and real peoples; and the evaluative kind — where a novel claims only the third. Genre labels settle less than readers assume in either direction: fiction does not forfeit truth, as seventy years of Golding's readers show, and non-fiction does not secure it; a label announces what a book claims, never what it achieves. Whether the Bible's history happened as written has been argued for centuries and is argued today, seriously, on both sides. This essay is not entering those arguments; its own argument rides the third kind only.

It does not have to. That is what the fiction example buys, and it is all it buys. If a book in which nothing happened can hand you something true, then a book whose history is disputed can too. The reader who says "I will read it as nothing but myth until every sentence is certified" is applying a standard no book on his shelf could survive, and the cost of that standard is not paid by the book. It is paid by the reader, in everything the book would have said to him.

What the comparison does, and does not, concede

Heard one way, setting Scripture beside fiction concedes that Scripture is fiction. Nothing here concedes that. The argument runs on a floor, not a ceiling: even from the most skeptical seat in the house, the seat where the whole book is Golding, the text's capacity to say true things survives. I do not sit in that seat. I believe the book's central claims are true in the strongest sense of the word. But the essay's argument deliberately does not borrow that belief, and a reader who rejects it loses nothing of the invitation.

Alexander Pope named the hazard of the shallow verdict three centuries ago, in lines so worn by quotation that their second half is usually lost: "A little learning is a dangerous thing / Drink deep, or taste not the Pierian spring / There shallow draughts intoxicate the brain, / And drinking largely sobers us again." The spring belonged to the Muses; the counsel survives the mythology. A shallow draught of this book — a verse carried off to win an argument, a hard chapter skimmed and dismissed, a story half-remembered from childhood — intoxicates: it leaves the reader certain he has tasted the whole. Pope's remedy is not abstinence. It is depth.

The book makes the same turn in its own voice, from warning to welcome:

Psalm 34:8 (NRSVue)

PS 34:8O taste and see that the LORD is good; happy are those who take refuge in him.

And the book contains, near its end, the scene this essay has been circling. A Roman governor stands in front of a prisoner who has just said, "Everyone who belongs to the truth listens to my voice" (John 18:37, NRSVue):

John 18:38 (NRSVue)

JN 18:38Pilate asked him, "What is truth?" After he had said this, he went out to the Jews again and told them, "I find no case against him."

Pilate asked the question with the answer standing in front of him, and did not wait. What this text claims about truth in that scene is as strong a claim as any book has made. The essay does not ask you to believe it. It asks you not to walk out of the room before you have read it.

Where This Lands

You do not need a verdict on everything in this book before it can say something true to you. Fiction settles that: did it happen and is it true are different questions. And the posture the reading asks of you is one you have held before: acceptance before understanding, the price every framework charges, the one your feet pay gravity with every step. Everything beyond that modest point, everything I actually believe about this book, waits inside it. The invitation is the whole conclusion: read it.

Conclusion

From Inside

At the end, my belief belongs in full view. I believe the claim John makes in his opening lines, and I believe it is the answer Pilate did not wait for:

John 1:1, 14  ·  John 14:6 (NRSVue)

JN 1:1In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

JN 1:14And the Word became flesh and lived among us, and we have seen his glory, the glory as of a father's only son, full of grace and truth.

JN 14:6Jesus said to him, "I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."

I entered this framework the way this essay has described every framework being entered: not with proof already in hand, but with a trust that preceded full understanding and has deepened under every honest examination since. I cannot hand that to you from outside.

For the skeptic: I am not trying to argue you into faith, and I am not asking you to believe any of it. Give the text a chance. Read it the way you read Golding: open to being handed something true by a book you have not yet decided to trust. Start with a Gospel; Mark is short. What you conclude once inside is yours.

For those who already believe: the invitation runs in the other direction, and it costs more. If this book is true, and your faith rests on it, then the flow must run from the text to the faith: the book informing what you believe, not your beliefs deciding in advance what the book is allowed to say. That order is easy to affirm and hard to keep, because every reader arrives with a version already in hand. I have found that the passages that complicated my theology were the ones that deepened it, and that the discomfort is usually where the text is actually speaking. Reading it honestly means reading all of it, including the parts that do not fit the version you brought.

Pilate's question still stands at the center of the book, asked by a man looking directly at the answer, who left before he could hear it. The book is still open. That is the whole invitation.

Criticisms

Arguments Against This Invitation

Here are the strongest objections to what this essay proposes.

An invitation is not an argument. The essay establishes something nobody disputes, that fiction can carry truth, and declines to establish anything anybody does dispute. The author's real position, that the book is true in the strong sense, is carried entirely by testimony. A reader can grant every sentence here and owe nothing more at the end than he owed at the beginning. A critic may fairly judge that a claim this small was not worth an essay, and nothing here refutes that judgment.

The comparison gives the skeptic a comfortable place to stop. "True the way Golding is true" is a stable resting point: a reader can settle there, moved and enriched and unconverted, and nothing in this essay can dislodge him. The author's denial that the Bible is fiction rests on a confession the essay deliberately does not defend. The invitation may therefore deliver readers to the very shelf the author believes is the wrong one, and the essay has no answer to that beyond its own hope.

Recognition is weak evidence. The consistency with which readers "recognize" truth in stories could be shared evolutionary psychology rather than contact with anything real: we are wired to respond to tales of cooperation, sacrifice, and betrayal because those responses were adaptive. On that account fiction carries not truths but triggers, and the essay's third kind of truth, the evaluative kind, is a compliment we pay to whatever moves us. The essay's working sense asserts otherwise; it does not prove otherwise, and it says so.

The mathematics can only be an analogy, and analogies can flatter. The Bible is not a formal system. Arithmetic's rules are explicit and finite and its truths are provable inside it; Scripture's rules, genre and covenant and idiom, are recovered by interpretation, and interpreters dispute them. Setting the two side by side describes a similar posture of reading; it cannot certify that the postures are genuinely alike, and a skeptic may fairly suspect that arithmetic is on the page to lend Scripture a rigor the essay has not earned. The essay's stated intention, that the mathematics describes an act and supports no conclusion, is an intention, not a demonstration that the borrowing has been avoided.

The pattern is stretched across unlike things. Gravity compels the body; arithmetic compels any mind that grants its axioms; moral recognition compels neither, and a reader can decline it without contradiction. The essay says so, and asks for the weakest kind of acceptance by name — but naming the difference does not dissolve the sequence's momentum. The chapters still move from the undeniable toward the contested, and a reader may arrive at the invitation carrying credit the strong members earned. If the borrowed momentum does the persuading, the invitation inherits it.

The invitation is not unique to this book. Acceptance-before-understanding is how every framework is entered, so every framework can extend the same invitation on the same terms. Any scripture, any philosophy charges an entry price and promises understanding from inside; nothing in this essay supplies a criterion for why this book's price is the one worth paying, and the essay's own design forbids it one, because a criterion would be evidence and the evidence has been forsworn. A reader with one evening and many books may fairly ask why this one, and the essay's only answer is its author's confession — which is not a criterion.
