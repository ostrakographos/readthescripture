On Truth — An Invitation to Read

Some things in a book can be true even when not everything in it is. An essay on fact, framework, story, and Scripture; less an argument than an invitation.

Study
≈11 min read Challenging On reading Foundational
Abstract

This essay separates the question did it happen? from the question is it true?, watches how truths are reached inside arithmetic, tests the distinction on fiction, from Golding's island to the parables, and brings it to the Bible. It argues one narrow claim and closes with an invitation rather than a proof.

Written from an orthodox Christian confession

Introduction

A Smaller Claim Than You Might Expect

Essays that put "truth" and "the Bible" in the same sentence usually reach next for evidence: manuscripts, archaeology, fulfilled prophecy, argument stacked on argument. This one will not. Not because the evidence embarrasses me, but because the claim I want to make does not need it, and reaching for it would disguise what kind of claim it is.

The claim is this: some things in a book can be true even if not everything in the book is true. That is nearly the whole of what this essay argues. It is a modest claim, and most readers already grant it freely for every book on their shelf except one. Reaching it takes one detour through arithmetic, not to borrow its authority but because mathematics turns out to be the plainest picture of the one small act this essay asks of you.

Where I stand belongs on the table before we start. I am a Christian. I believe this book is true in a far stronger sense than the sentence above claims, and the rest of this site says so openly. But that belief is mine, and it is not the argument here. What follows does not attempt a proof and does not want to be one. It is an invitation: to read the book, with the same courtesy you extend to every other book that has ever told you something true.

The Question

Distinguishing Truth from Fact, Observation, Opinion

Truth needs to be distinguished from three things it is often confused with. A fact is a verifiable, measurable claim: water boils at 100°C at sea level. Facts can be confirmed or falsified, and they depend on instruments, definitions, and reference points. An observation is what a perceiver records at a moment, real but limited; the same sky looks different to different eyes. An opinion is a judgment: that act was unjust, this painting is beautiful. Opinions are not facts. But notice the interesting case: "that child's punishment was unjust" cannot be measured, yet if the child was genuinely innocent and genuinely punished, the injustice exists whether or not anyone holds the opinion about it. An opinion can track something real that is not reducible to measurement.

Even the largest truth we hold, that the Universe exists, we hold without being able to describe most of what it contains. Dark energy accounts for roughly 68% of its total energy content, dark matter for another 27%, and the visible, star-filled cosmos we actually know amounts to about 5% of the whole. How much does the Universe weigh? A fair question with no answer: weight needs gravity, gravity needs a reference point, and the Universe has no exterior to be weighed from. Time bends the same way, running slower near massive objects, a feature of the thing itself rather than a fixed backdrop behind it. The certainty that the Universe is does not wait on an account of what it weighs, when it began, or what most of it is made of. We hold truths, comfortably and every day, that we cannot fully describe.

A Working Sense of Truth

Truth, as used here, exists independently of whether anyone has stated it, measured it, witnessed it, or agreed to it. Consensus does not define it, and observation does not generate it. The Universe must exist for us to be discussing it. Justice must be a real standard for injustice to be a real wrong. Love must be real for its absence to be felt as loss rather than mere preference.

We are not claiming to have fully described Truth. We are claiming it exists, and that our partial, imperfect access to it is worth taking seriously.

The Word Underneath "True"

English "true" leans toward accuracy: a true sentence is one that matches the facts. The Hebrew word underneath most of the Bible's talk of truth leans elsewhere. Truth (אֶמֶת | ʾĕmet | Heb - H571) comes from the root ʾāman (H539), to support, to make firm; the standard academic lexicon (Brown-Driver-Briggs) glosses it "firmness, faithfulness, truth." The same root gives ʾĕmûnāh, "faithfulness," and āmēn, "truly." In this vocabulary a true word is not first a word that matches; it is a word that holds, one you can put weight on. So when the Psalmist says "the sum of your word is truth" (Ps 119:160, NRSVue), the claim is about reliability before it is about inventory. The Bible's own truth-language is a reliability vocabulary before it is an accuracy vocabulary, and God's faithfulness and his truth are cousins from the same root.

Frameworks

You Must Accept the Framework Before the Truth Within It

Truths are understood within frameworks, and the framework has to be accepted before the truths inside it come within reach. That is not a weakness of human knowledge; it is the structure of human knowledge, and mathematics shows the structure more plainly than anywhere else.

Start with a truth that does not feel true. Most people's first reaction to 0.999… = 1 is that it cannot be right: surely the endless string of nines creeps toward 1 forever and falls just short forever. But inside standard arithmetic, where an infinite decimal means the limit of the sequence it names, the two are not approximately equal. They are the same number, exactly and necessarily. No amount of standing outside arithmetic resolves the discomfort, because the discomfort never came from the math; it came from the standing outside. You accept the system, you work inside it, and the truth becomes reachable. Notice what was never required: you do not have to like the rule about infinite decimals, then or ever. Acceptance is not agreement. It is the price of admission, and it is paid before understanding, not after.

There is a deeper fact about that admission price: inside mathematics it is never refunded. In 1931 Kurt Gödel proved that any consistent formal system rich enough to do ordinary arithmetic contains true statements it cannot prove, and that among the statements it cannot prove is its own consistency. To certify that arithmetic will never contradict itself, you must stand in a stronger system, which cannot certify itself either, and so on upward without a top. Every working mathematician therefore operates inside a framework whose own soundness is trusted without proof, permanently. Mathematics is not weaker for this, and no one mourns it; it is simply what working inside a system is. The most rigorous framework human beings have ever built runs on a trust it cannot generate for itself, and it works gloriously anyway.

The Same Posture in Physics

Physicists working inside quantum mechanics sometimes call their posture "shut up and calculate": accept the mathematical framework and use it productively, even though no one fully agrees on what it means at the level of physical reality. The framework earns its trust not by being proven from outside but by producing coherent, reliable results inside. That is a different kind of trust than mathematical proof, and it is not irrational. It is the working scientist's daily form of acceptance before understanding.

Said once, plainly: none of this makes the Bible more likely to be true, and none of it is on the page to do that. The arithmetic is here to describe an act, the act of reading from inside a system you have provisionally accepted; it is not here to lend anything its authority. What the description removes is exactly one excuse: the idea that accepting-before-understanding is a peculiar demand religion makes and rigorous people refuse. It is how every framework is entered, including the most rigorous one there is. And entering a framework changes your access to its truths, not their status as truths. What stays outside when you stay outside is not the truth; it is your reach.

An Author's Comment — Opinion, Not Description

Here is the nearest picture I have, offered as my own proposal and nothing more: the Bible is some kind of formula. I mean that in the sense this section has been developing — a text with internal rules (genre, covenant, idiom, a truth-word that means what holds) that yields its meaning only to a reader who provisionally accepts those rules while reading, much as arithmetic does. You do not have to understand all of mathematics to learn from it, and you do not have to agree with every rule; but for it to work, you have to accept it. I think the same is true of this book. "Some kind of" is doing real work in that sentence: a formula is a picture, not a definition, and some believers will find the picture too cold while some skeptics will find it too generous. I keep it because it names exactly what I am asking of a reader — not belief, not agreement, just acceptance long enough to read.

Story

What Fiction Proves

In 1954 William Golding published Lord of the Flies. None of it happened. There was no island, no stranded schoolboys, no conch, no signal fire, no Piggy. By the accuracy standard, every sentence in the book is false. And yet readers have come away from it for seventy years knowing something true: that the order we live inside is thinner than it looks, that cruelty does not need to be taught, that the beast the boys hunt in the forest is not out in the trees. No reader withholds that recognition until the island's coordinates are produced. The two questions, did it happen and is it true, come apart the moment you hold them side by side, and nobody actually reads as though they were one question.

The pattern repeats wherever stories do their deepest work. To Kill a Mockingbird carries a truth about justice without arguing it: Atticus Finch does everything right and loses, the jury convicts a man it knows is innocent, and the story offers no consoling ending. It simply makes the injustice specific and felt, and in doing so it appeals to something in the reader that already knows what justice requires even when justice is not delivered. In Charlotte's Web, Charlotte saves Wilbur at the cost of her own strength and dies alone at the fair, having asked for nothing in return; children and adults alike recognize in her what love actually is when it is fully expressed. In the film Arrival, Louise Banks learns her daughter will die young and chooses to have her anyway: knowing the ending in advance does not change whether the story is worth having.

These recognitions resist explanation more stubbornly than mathematical truth does. We cannot write an equation for love or run a repeatable experiment on belonging. Yet the recognition is remarkably consistent across cultures, ages, and centuries; the same stories move people who share no language, religion, or era. We cannot fully say why these things are true, and we recognize them anyway. Fiction, where nothing happened at all, turns out to be one of the most reliable carriers of truth we have.

Novelists did not discover this pattern. The Bible's own shelf has practiced it from the beginning. Job opens as a framed tale, a man in the land of Uz and a heavenly wager, and spends most of its length as a poetry debate about innocent suffering; readers in every century have met their own anguish in it, and none of that meeting has ever waited on producing Uz on a map. Proverbs collects observations that verify themselves in use. Ecclesiastes still lands after more than two thousand years precisely because it declines to hand the reader a system. And at the center of the Gospels, Jesus teaches by deliberate fiction: the parables. The good Samaritan does not have to have existed for Luke 10 to be true; no listener, then or now, has escaped "Go and do likewise" (Luke 10:37, NRSVue) by asking for the Samaritan's address. Whatever a reader has or has not concluded about the Gospels' history, the parables inside them already work the way Golding works. The book this essay invites you to read has been making this point on its own pages all along.

The Text

The Bible Is Not Lord of the Flies

Here the essay has to be careful in both directions. The Bible is not a novel. It is a library: history and law, poetry and proverb, prophecy, wisdom, letters, apocalypse, composed across roughly a thousand years and tethered throughout to real places and real peoples. And it claims what no novel claims: that God has spoken and acted in history. Whether its history happened as written has been argued for centuries and is argued today, seriously, on both sides. This essay is not entering those arguments.

It does not have to. That is what the fiction example buys, and it is all it buys. If a book in which nothing happened can hand you something true, then a book whose history is disputed can too. The reader who says "I will read it as nothing but myth until every sentence is certified" is applying a standard no book on his shelf could survive, and the cost of that standard is not paid by the book. It is paid by the reader, in everything the book would have said to him.

What the comparison does, and does not, concede

Heard one way, setting Scripture beside fiction concedes that Scripture is fiction. Nothing here concedes that. The argument runs on a floor, not a ceiling: even from the most skeptical seat in the house, the seat where the whole book is Golding, the text's capacity to say true things survives. I do not sit in that seat. I believe the book's central claims are true in the strongest sense of the word. But the essay's argument deliberately does not borrow that belief, and a reader who rejects it loses nothing of the invitation.

The book, as it happens, extends the same invitation in its own voice. Its truth-word, as we saw, is the reliability word; and its psalmist does not say "verify and see":

Psalm 34:8 (NRSVue)

PS 34:8O taste and see that the LORD is good; happy are those who take refuge in him.

The figure is exact. Taste is the one kind of knowing that cannot be done on someone else's report.

And the book contains, near its end, the scene this essay has been circling. A Roman governor stands in front of a prisoner who has just said, "Everyone who belongs to the truth listens to my voice" (John 18:37, NRSVue):

John 18:38 (NRSVue)

JO 18:38Pilate asked him, "What is truth?" After he had said this, he went out to the Jews again and told them, "I find no case against him."

Pilate asked the question with the answer standing in front of him, and did not wait. Earlier in the same Gospel, Jesus had said, "I am the way, and the truth, and the life" (John 14:6). Truth, in this text's final claim, is not a property of sentences; it has a name, a face, and a history. That is as strong a claim as any book makes about truth. The essay does not ask you to believe it. It asks you not to walk out of the room before you have read it.

Where This Lands

You do not need a verdict on everything in this book before it can say something true to you. Fiction settles that: did it happen and is it true are different questions. And the posture the reading asks of you is one you have held before: acceptance before understanding, the same coin every framework charges. Everything beyond that modest point, everything I actually believe about this book, waits inside it. The invitation is the whole conclusion: read it.

Conclusion

From Inside

At the end, my belief belongs in full view. I believe the claim John makes in his opening lines, and I believe it is the answer Pilate did not wait for:

John 1:1, 14  ·  John 14:6 (NRSVue)

JN 1:1In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

JN 1:14And the Word became flesh and lived among us, and we have seen his glory, the glory as of a father's only son, full of grace and truth.

JN 14:6Jesus said to him, "I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."

I entered this framework the way this essay has described every framework being entered: not with proof already in hand, but with a trust that preceded full understanding and has deepened under every honest examination since. I cannot hand that to you from outside, and this essay has not tried.

For the skeptic: I am not trying to argue you into faith, and I am not asking you to believe any of it. Give the text a chance. Read it the way you read Golding, expecting to be told the truth by an author you have not yet decided to trust. Start with a Gospel; Mark is short. What you conclude once inside is yours.

For those who already believe: the truths in this text are not protected by avoiding its difficult parts. I have found the opposite. The passages that complicated my theology were the ones that deepened it, and the discomfort is usually where the text is actually speaking. Reading it honestly means reading all of it, including the parts that do not fit the version you brought to it.

Pilate's question still stands at the center of the book, asked by a man looking directly at the answer, who left before he could hear it. The book is still open. That is the whole invitation.

Criticisms

Arguments Against This Invitation

Any argument that cannot survive its own criticism is not worth making. Here are the strongest objections to what this essay proposes.

An invitation is not an argument. The essay establishes something nobody disputes, that fiction can carry truth, and declines to establish anything anybody does dispute. The author's real position, that the book is true in the strong sense, is carried entirely by testimony. A reader can grant every sentence here and owe nothing more at the end than he owed at the beginning. A critic may fairly judge that a claim this small was not worth an essay, and nothing here refutes that judgment.

The comparison gives the skeptic a comfortable place to stop. "True the way Golding is true" is a stable resting point: a reader can settle there, moved and enriched and unconverted, and nothing in this essay can dislodge him. The author's denial that the Bible is fiction rests on a confession the essay deliberately does not defend. The invitation may therefore deliver readers to precisely the shelf the author believes is the wrong one. That risk stands.

Recognition is weak evidence. The consistency with which readers "recognize" truth in stories could be shared evolutionary psychology rather than contact with anything real: we are wired to respond to tales of cooperation, sacrifice, and betrayal because those responses were adaptive. On that account fiction carries not truths but triggers, and the essay's word "true" is a compliment we pay to whatever moves us. The essay's working sense of Truth asserts otherwise; it does not prove otherwise. That stands too.

The mathematics can only be an analogy, and analogies can flatter. The Bible is not a formal system. Arithmetic's rules are explicit and finite and its truths are provable inside it; Scripture's rules, genre and covenant and idiom, are recovered by interpretation, and interpreters dispute them. Setting the two side by side describes a similar posture of reading; it cannot certify that the postures are genuinely alike, and a skeptic may fairly suspect that arithmetic is on the page to lend Scripture a rigor the essay has not earned. The essay's stated intention, that the mathematics describes an act and supports no conclusion, is an intention, not a demonstration that the borrowing has been avoided. That suspicion stands.
