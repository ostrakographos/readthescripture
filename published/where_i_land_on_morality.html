Where I Land on Morality

Good and harm are real, and anyone can see them. The systems built on top of them are human. What that leaves a believer to do with the word "moral" — held as opinion.

Marginalia

Marginalia  ·  My own take, not a graded study. Notes in the margin of the text. Here's my bias, and here's where I could be wrong.

≈6 min read Moderate Morality · a personal landing
Where I'm coming from

I write from inside the Christian confession this site states openly, and this page is the one place I set down what I think about morality itself — as opinion, not argument. I believe good and harm are real and that anyone can see them. I doubt that any human moral system, mine included, is the territory it maps. Where I can't resolve something, I've left it open.

I came to moral philosophy late, and it felt like discovering programming for the first time. There are languages, paradigms, platforms, editors, and a thousand strong opinions about which is best. What surprised me wasn't the number of options. It was the heat: people hold their frameworks the way some engineers hold object-oriented programming — as a religion with a style guide. It's not that I lack a framework — I have one, and it may get its own study in time — but this page isn't selling it. This page is where I set down what I actually think, labeled as opinion.

What I'm sure of

The territory

Start with the part I'm sure of. Cruelty is wrong and kindness is good, and I have never needed a theory to see either. Good is real — a meal that feeds, a bridge that holds, a life kept alive. Harm is just as real, and I don't need to stage a thought experiment to prove it. And none of it is gated behind belief: believers and unbelievers see good, do good, and suffer harm every day of their lives. Whatever else on this page is uncertain, that isn't.

One more thing I'm sure of, running the other direction. Some choices are forced: every road out destroys something real. When that happens, the systems are quick to hand back a clean word — permissible, justified, net-positive — and I have stopped trusting the word. A death that had to be chosen is still a death; the loss does not become tidy because the alternative was worse. If there is a least-wrong option, taking it can be right, and what it costs stays on the books. Ezekiel has God say it plainly: "For I have no pleasure in the death of anyone" (Ezek 18:32). I take that to include the necessary ones.

What I'm not sure of

The maps

What I'm unsure of is everything built on top: the systems, the isms, the rulebooks. Three possibilities seem live to me. Either we can't fully know what morality is; or we can only partially understand and describe it, the way physics partially describes the world; or it doesn't exist as an objective thing at all — no compass, only conventions. I lean toward the middle one: the descriptions are human, the thing described is real. What inclines me against full objectivity is that I have never met two people who share one whole moral map. I know disagreement alone doesn't settle it — people disagreed about the shape of the earth without the earth losing one — so this stays a lean, not a finding.

It's also why I use the word "morality" reluctantly. To my ear it names the map, not the territory — the label humans put on the good and bad of things so they can be argued, codified, and enforced. Where I can, I'll say good and harm for the territory, and wisdom or discernment for the faculty that reads it; "morality" appears on this page the way a specimen appears in a study — as the thing under examination.

If morality is objective, then I think it is God's — and we understand it about as well as we understand God, which is to say partially, at a distance, through knowledge that strains. Two things follow, and they pull in opposite directions. First: I can't treat it as nothing, because I believe its ground is real. Second: I can't wield it.

There is no rulebook in my hands, and Scripture never claims to be one. Right and wrong can be learned from it — from the commands, from God's own character, and from its unflinching anti-examples (the book of Judges reads like a warning label). But it legislates far less than it could, deliberately as far as I can tell, and it assigns the remainder to wisdom: Solomon, with the Law already in his possession, still asks for "an understanding mind… able to discern between good and evil" (1 Kgs 3:9); Hebrews expects "faculties… trained by practice to distinguish good from evil" (Heb 5:14); James says whoever lacks wisdom should ask God, "who gives to all generously" (Jas 1:5). Asked for, practiced, given — and how much of it can be built rather than simply dealt, the way intelligence or height is dealt, I don't claim to know. What I do know is that a judgment which has to be discerned is a judgment I can't wield over anyone. If someone tells me they have completed the codebook, the claim itself is the tell. Scripture talks about the good in a register no codebook uses:

"He has told you, O mortal, what is good, and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice and to love kindness and to walk humbly with your God?"

Micah 6:8 · NRSVue

Told — not proven, and not codified into a metric. What is good gets announced to a mortal, who is then asked to walk humbly. That is a strange closing instruction to attach to a rulebook, and a natural one to attach to a relationship.

The uncomfortable part

Judgment, and the orchard

I don't believe I'm entitled to judge people outside my faith by a standard they never took up. Paul says as much: "For what have I to do with judging those outside?" — and his next question, "Are you not judges of those who are inside?" (1 Cor 5:12), hands the church a judging job within its own walls. I hesitate even there, which puts me a step short of where Paul stands; maybe he is right and I am timid. I've marked the gap rather than resolved it. I do take some company from the Lord himself, who kept finding what he was looking for on the far side of the line his people drew: "not even in Israel have I found such faith," he says of a Roman centurion (Luke 7:9), and to a Canaanite mother, "Woman, great is your faith!" (Matt 15:28).

What I keep noticing instead is how the commands run when they touch the people I'd be tempted to measure. The harvest laws tell Israel not to reap to the very edges of the field and not to strip the vineyard bare, but to leave the gleanings for the poor and the alien — the stranger passing through (Lev 19:9–10). The same chapter goes further: the alien "shall be to you as the native-born among you; you shall love the alien as yourself" (Lev 19:33–34). I am instructed to leave part of my orchard unharvested, on purpose, for people I will never vet — and then to love them as myself. The posture commanded toward the outsider is provision, not verdict.

And then there is the word the whole faith travels under: news. Good news. News is what you carry about something that has already happened. You don't argue a person into news; you announce it. I suspect that is the point. Death and sin were dealt with somewhere I was not consulted, and what remains to each person is to accept or decline. So the sharing is meant to look like rejoicing, proclamation, and instruction for whoever wants it — a town crier's job, not a magistrate's. Every time I have watched the good news carried as a verdict instead, it stopped being news and became a sentence. Sentencing was never delegated to me.

What this costs

Where I could be wrong

Holding this costs me things I would like to have. The moral argument for God — objective morality exists; only God explains it; therefore God — is off my table, because my own account says the observation of good is available to everyone and the grounding is something I trust rather than know. I can't deploy as public proof what I hold as testimony. From the other side, philosophers like Derek Parfit and Philippa Foot argue that objective morality stands with no God under it at all; if they are right, my "if it's objective, it's God's" is wrong, and that possibility keeps its seat here. Hume's old gap — no ought from an is — goes unanswered on this page too; nothing here crosses it.

There is also a suspicion worth naming, because someone will: this position conveniently disarms the people I most disagree with — the ones who use morality as a gate and a gavel. All I can offer against the suspicion is that it disarms me first, and that it costs me the argument I would most like to use. I hold one further suspicion loosely: that Genesis 3 describes this very seizure — that "knowing good and evil" names the taking of the gauge into our own hands. But that is a study I have not done yet, and I won't pretend I have.

So: good and harm are real, and everyone can see them. The systems that organize them are human, and nobody's system is the territory. If there is an objective moral order, it is God's, known the way God is known — partially, in trust. Even Abraham, the one time he stood before the Judge of all the earth to argue a verdict, argued it downward — "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?" (Gen 18:25) is a question asked to spare a city, not to sentence one. What that leaves me to do with the word this page examines is mostly what the harvest law and the good news already said: provide, announce, rejoice, instruct when asked, and leave the verdicts to the one qualified to render them. I hold all of this the way this page is labeled — as opinion, open to correction from either direction.
