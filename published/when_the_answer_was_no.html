When the Answer Was No

Moses at the border, David on the floor, the healed man at the boat, Paul with the thorn, Christ in the garden — the prayers God answered by refusing, and what the faithful did next

Study
≈16 min read Moderate Thematic study Pastoral
Abstract

Scripture records prayers of the faithful that God answered by refusing: Moses pleading to cross the Jordan, David fasting for a dying child, David's offer to build the temple, Elijah asking to die, the healed man of Gerasa begging to go with Jesus, Paul asking three times for the thorn to leave, and Jesus asking for the cup to pass. This study reads the recorded denials side by side — what was asked, how the no was spoken, and what the text shows each person doing after it — and weighs how much pattern the cases will actually bear.

Written from an orthodox Christian confession.

This is the third panel of a set. When Faith Moves gathers prayers that drew a yes; When the Answer Was Silence holds the cries that met nothing at all. This study takes the remaining case — the prayer that was heard, weighed, and refused. Each piece stands on its own; together they are one argument about what the Bible actually records.
Introduction

A no is not a silence

The hardest prayers in Scripture are usually sorted into one bin marked unanswered. The texts are more precise than that. Job's problem is that God will not speak; the psalmist of the eighty-eighth psalm goes down into darkness with no reply at all. But Moses got a reply. So did David, twice; so did Elijah, a healed man among the Gerasenes, Paul, and — on the worst night the Gospels record — Jesus. Each of them asked for something specific, and the reply was a refusal. A no is not a silence. It is an answer: the relationship holds, the voice comes back, and what it says is no.

That distinction does real pastoral work, which is why this study exists as its own piece. Silence raises the question is anyone there? Denial raises a different and in some ways harder one: someone is there, heard me, could have — and said no. The believer who has lived through both knows they do not feel the same, and Scripture does not treat them the same. The denied prayers come with speech, with presence, sometimes with a reason, and — in nearly every case this study examines — with something put into the empty hands. Whether that last observation is a pattern or a coincidence of the canon's selection, and which case it cannot cover, are questions held open until the end.

A word on where this piece stands. It is written from the site's confession: the writer believes these refusals were the acts of a God who was present in them, not literary devices about an absent one. That belief is stated here once so the reader can weigh it, and the cases below are laid out so that a reader who does not share it can still check every text.

One · David and the child

The grammar of petition: "Who knows?"

Begin with the starkest case, because it supplies the grammar for all the others. The child born of David's adultery with Bathsheba falls sick, and Nathan has already told David the child will die (2 Sam 12:14). David pleads anyway: he fasts, wears sackcloth, and lies all night on the ground for seven days (12:16–18). The servants are afraid to tell him the child has died — they expect grief to break him. Instead the text gives one of the strangest sequences of verbs in the David story: he rises, washes, anoints himself, changes clothes, goes into the house of the LORD, worships, returns home, and asks for bread (12:20). The servants ask the question the reader is asking. His answer:

"While the child was still alive, I fasted and wept, for I said, 'Who knows? The LORD may be gracious to me, and the child may live.' But now he is dead; why should I fast? Can I bring him back again? I shall go to him, but he will not return to me."

2 Samuel 12:22–23 · NRSVue

"Who knows?" — who knows (מִי יוֹדֵעַ | mî yôdēaʿ | Heb - H4310 + H3045) — turns out to be a fixed formula of biblical prayer, and it is worth pausing on, because it defines the posture this whole study examines. The king of Nineveh uses it when he calls his city to repent: "Who knows? God may relent and change his mind" (Jonah 3:9). The prophet Joel uses it verbatim when he calls Israel to return: "Who knows whether he will not turn and relent" (Joel 2:14).1 The formula concedes, inside the prayer itself, that the outcome is not owed. David fasts seven days on a who knows — petition at full strength, presumption at zero. When the no lands, it finds nothing in his prayer that has to shatter, because the prayer never claimed a guarantee. What it claimed was a hearing, and it got one.

Two details in the aftermath resist a cynical reading. The first verb after the no is worship — David's first recorded act on learning the child is dead is directed toward God, not away from him. And the sentence he speaks over the loss — "I shall go to him, but he will not return to me" — keeps the relationship's whole horizon in view without pretending the loss is smaller than it is. The child stays dead. The fast ends; the mourning customs run backward; the text neither praises nor explains him. It simply shows what petition under mî yôdēaʿ looks like when the answer comes back no: the asking ends, and the worship does not.

Two · Moses at the border

The refused prayer the synagogue named a Sabbath for

Moses tells the story of his own denial in the first person. At the end of forty years, with the land in sight, he asks for the one thing left that he wants:

"At that time, too, I entreated the LORD, saying, 'O Lord GOD, you have only begun to show your servant your greatness and your might. What god in heaven or on earth can perform deeds and mighty acts like yours? Let me cross over to see the good land beyond the Jordan, that good hill country and the Lebanon.' But the LORD was angry with me on your account and would not heed me. The LORD said to me, 'Enough from you! Never speak to me of this matter again!'"

Deuteronomy 3:23–26 · NRSVue

The opening verb — I entreated, pleaded for grace (וָאֶתְחַנַּן | wāʾetḥannan | Heb - H2603) — is the reflexive form of ḥānan, the root of "grace": to plead for favor one has no claim to.2 Moses, of all people, prays like a man with no leverage. And the synagogue noticed something the church rarely has: the weekly Torah reading that opens with this verse is named for it — Va'etchanan, "and I pleaded."3 Every year, congregations name a Sabbath after a prayer that was refused. The tradition that preserved this text did not find the refusal embarrassing; it found it worth rehearsing.

The refusal itself is abrupt to the point of harshness: enough for you (רַב־לָךְ | rab-lāk | Heb - H7227) — "Enough from you!" — with an order never to raise the matter again.4 This is the model of a no with no soft edges. And yet the same speech continues into three gifts that are easy to read past: go up Pisgah and look in every direction — the sight of the land, granted to the eyes that will not cross into it (3:27); charge Joshua, encourage him — the work handed on rather than ended (3:28); and, when the moment comes, a death scene unlike any other in Scripture: Moses dies on the mountain "at the LORD's command," his sight unimpaired and his vigor unabated, and God buries him (Deut 34:5–7). The no stands to the end — "you shall not cross over there" (34:4) — spoken over the whole land spread out below the man who asked.

The border was not the first limit Moses had asked against. Years earlier, at Sinai, he had asked for something greater than the land: "Please show me your glory" (Exod 33:18). The refusal came wrapped in a promise — his goodness would pass before him, his name would be proclaimed — and then the line that marks the edge of every mortal asking: "you cannot see my face, for no one shall see me and live" (33:20). What follows is the gentlest denial in the Torah: a place on the rock, a cleft, "I will cover you with my hand until I have passed by," and then — "you shall see my back" (33:22–23). He asked for the face and was given the back: not what he asked, and more than anyone else in the story is ever shown. The same chapter has already said that the LORD used to speak to Moses "face to face, as one speaks to a friend" (33:11); the seam between that sentence and this refusal is the chapter's own, and this study leaves it as the chapter leaves it. Twice, then, the man with the most access in the Torah asked past its boundary — the glory, then the land — and twice the boundary held, and both times something was set into his hands on the near side of it.

Why was Moses refused? The accounts differ

Numbers gives a cause: at Meribah, Moses struck the rock and, with Aaron, "did not trust in me, to show my holiness before the eyes of the Israelites" — "therefore you shall not bring this assembly into the land" (Num 20:12). Deuteronomy, in Moses' own retellings, points the blame elsewhere: "the LORD was angry with me on your account" (Deut 3:26; similarly 1:37; 4:21). One text names Moses' act; the other has Moses name the people. Harmonizations exist (the people's provocation occasioned Moses' failure), and source-critical readings take the two as different traditions with different theologies of the wilderness generation. This study does not adjudicate. It flags the seam because the seam matters to the argument: if Scripture's own accounts frame the reason for this no differently, then confidence about the reason for any particular no — including one's own — should be held loosely. The refusal is on the record twice; the explanation does not hold still.

One more scene belongs to this case, and it is offered as the author's observation rather than the text's claim. In the Gospels, Moses appears once more — on a mountain inside the land, speaking with Jesus at the transfiguration (Matt 17:3). The Torah's no was final on its own terms, and nothing in Matthew presents the scene as its reversal. But a reader who has just left Moses on Nebo, looking across at hills he will never stand on, is permitted to notice where the tradition next places his feet.

Three · David and the house

The no that came back larger

Not every recorded denial refuses a desperate prayer; one refuses a generous offer. It keeps its place in this set all the same: an offer laid before God is still a petition — something asked, that heaven may decline — and the refusal that answers it has the same anatomy as the others. Settled in his palace, David proposes to build God a house — a temple to replace the tent. Nathan, reasonably, blesses the plan. That night the word of the LORD arrives to overrule them both:

"You shall not build me a house to live in."

1 Chronicles 17:4 · NRSVue

Samuel's version puts it as a question — "Are you the one to build me a house to live in?" (2 Sam 7:5) — and then pivots on a wordplay the reader can watch happen: you will not build me a house; "the LORD will make you a house" (7:11). House as building becomes house as dynasty. The offspring will build the temple; the throne will be established forever (7:12–13). David's response is to go in, sit before the LORD, and pray a prayer that begins "Who am I, O Lord GOD…?" (7:18) — the refused builder responding to his refusal with the longest recorded thanksgiving of his life.

The reason for this no surfaces only decades later, in David's own farewell speech: "You shall not build a house for my name, for you are a warrior and have shed blood" (1 Chr 28:3). It is worth being precise about what that reason is not. It is not a condemnation — the same speeches affirm God's choice of David — and it is not a punishment attached to a sin, as Meribah was. It is a fit between the builder and the building: the house of rest would be built by the man of rest, Solomon, whose name shares its letters with shalom (1 Chr 22:9).5 Some prayers are refused not because the desire is wrong but because the desire is right and the person is wrong for it — a category most theologies of prayer have no shelf for, and the text states it without apology.

The Torah keeps one other refused offer, and it is the costliest in Scripture: Moses, after the golden calf, offering himself — if the people cannot be forgiven, "blot me out of the book that you have written." The answer is no: "Whoever has sinned against me I will blot out of my book" (Exod 32:32–33). The substitution is declined; each life answers for itself. That chapter — the intercession that moved God, and the offer he refused — has its own study on this site, and this page only points at it.

Four · Elijah and Jonah

Two prophets ask to die; neither is obliged

Twice, prophets pray for death, in so many words. Both prayers are refused, and the two refusals could not be delivered more differently.

"But he himself went a day's journey into the wilderness and came and sat down under a solitary broom tree. He asked that he might die, 'It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life, for I am no better than my ancestors.'"

1 Kings 19:4 · NRSVue

Elijah's prayer comes the day after his greatest public victory, with Jezebel's death-threat in his ears — exhausted, alone, and done. The refusal arrives with no speech at all at first: sleep, then a touch, then baked bread and a jar of water, then more sleep, then more food, "or the journey will be too much for you" (19:5–7). Only at Horeb, fed and rested, does the voice come — a question ("What are you doing here, Elijah?"), a theophany that locates God in a sound of sheer silence rather than the wind and fire, and then a commission with three anointings in it, one of which is Elijah's own successor (19:9–16). The prayer for an ending is answered with provisions for a continuing. And the man who asked to die is, in the end, the man the tradition records as never dying at all — taken up in the whirlwind (2 Kgs 2:11). No case in Scripture puts more distance between what was asked and what was given.

Jonah's death-wish is the same request from the opposite heart — not despair at failure but fury at success: Nineveh repented, God relented, "and now, O LORD, please take my life from me, for it is better for me to die than to live" (Jonah 4:3). This refusal is delivered as a question — "Is it right for you to be angry?" (4:4) — then as an object lesson with a bush, a worm, and a wind, then as a final question about mercy on which the book simply ends (4:9–11). Jonah is not fed by angels; he is argued with, patiently, and the text never tells us whether the argument landed. Two death-wishes, two refusals: one answered with breakfast, one with a question mark. What they share is what neither prophet gets — agreement — and what both get instead: a God who stays in the conversation the prophet was trying to leave.

Five · The other side of the lake

The man who asked to come along

Not every refused request is a plea against suffering. One of the strangest is a plea for company — and it draws the New Testament's only flat narration of Jesus refusing anyone. He has just delivered a man no chain could hold, in the region of the Gerasenes, on the far side of the lake; the man — clothed now, and in his right mind — asks for the one thing the reader expects the story to grant:9

"As he was getting into the boat, the man who had been possessed by demons begged him that he might be with him. But Jesus refused and said to him, 'Go home to your own people, and tell them how much the Lord has done for you and what mercy he has shown you.'"

Mark 5:18–19 · NRSVue

Weigh what was asked. "To be with him" is what the Twelve were appointed for (Mark 3:14); this man asks for it out of gratitude, on a shore whose people have just begged Jesus to leave. The request is devout, and the refusal is the narrator's own verb — Mark says refused, and gives no reason. What the no comes with is a sending. Go home; tell. And the man does it: "he went away and began to proclaim in the Decapolis how much Jesus had done for him, and everyone was amazed" (5:20; in Luke's telling, "declare how much God has done for you," 8:39). In a Gospel where the healed are ordered, again and again, to say nothing (Mark 1:44; 5:43; 7:36), this is the one man commanded to speak. He asked to be with Jesus; he was made Jesus's word in ten towns that had none. The no and the commission are one sentence — and of all the cases in this study, this is the only one where the text lets you watch the refused man spend the rest of his day doing the thing he was handed instead.

Six · Paul

The only no that comes with its reason attached

Every case so far leaves the refusal unexplained at the moment it lands, or explains it only years later. One case in Scripture hands the petitioner the rationale directly, in the refusal itself — and it is the case where the sufferer wrote the account in his own hand.

"Three times I appealed to the Lord about this, that it would leave me, but he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for power is made perfect in weakness.' So I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may dwell in me."

2 Corinthians 12:8–9 · NRSVue

What the thorn was — thorn, stake (σκόλοψ | skolops | Gk - G4647), "a thorn was given me in the flesh, a messenger of Satan to torment me" (12:7) — Paul never says, and the guesses (an eye disease, malaria, epilepsy, the relentless opponents of his ministry) have filled commentaries for two millennia without a verdict.6 The vagueness is probably the passage's pastoral genius: an identified thorn would belong to Paul; an unidentified one is lendable. What is specified is the arithmetic — three times — the same count the Gospels give Gethsemane, the number of a prayer pressed to its limit and then released.

The refusal has a shape worth stating exactly. It does not say the thorn is good. It does not say Paul misdiagnosed his need — the text agrees the thorn is "a messenger of Satan," and the appeal to remove it was reasonable. It says the request will not be granted and names what stands in its place: "my grace is sufficient" — is sufficient (ἀρκεῖ | arkei | Gk - G714), present tense, ongoing.7 The substitute for removal is supply. Paul's response goes a step further than acceptance — "I will boast all the more gladly" — which reads as either the most mature sentence a sufferer ever wrote or as rhetoric under pressure; the Criticism section below takes the second reading seriously. Either way, the structure of the case is the clearest in Scripture: heard, refused, told why, resourced, and left with the thorn still in.

Seven · Gethsemane

The model no

Every thread in this study runs through one garden. The petitioner is the Son; the request is specific and repeated three times; the theology of petition is stated inside the prayer itself:

"And going a little farther, he threw himself on the ground and prayed, 'My Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from me, yet not what I want but what you want.'"

Matthew 26:39 · NRSVue

"If it is possible" is the who knows posture in Greek dress — not the Hebrew formula translated, but the same concession built into the asking: petition at full strength, presumption at zero; David's grammar on the lips of David's son. The second clause — "yet not what I want but what you want" — is not resignation offered after the no, like David rising from the floor; it is consent built into the asking. The prayer is repeated with the same shape a second and third time (26:42–44), and then the answer arrives in the form of footsteps: "Look, my betrayer is at hand" (26:46). The cup does not pass. Of all the refusals in Scripture this is the barest — no Pisgah view, no dynasty, no bread under a broom tree, no sentence about sufficient grace. The Father's reply, within the scene itself, is the arrest.

The angel in the garden — a disputed verse

Luke's account adds a detail found in no other Gospel: "Then an angel from heaven appeared to him and gave him strength" (Luke 22:43–44, with the sweat like drops of blood). These two verses are absent from many of the earliest and best manuscripts, and modern critical editions bracket them as a probable early addition; the NRSVue prints them with a note.8 The detail is loved for an understandable reason — it would be the one moment where the refusing Father visibly sends something into the garden. Readers should know its textual footing is contested, and this study builds nothing on it.

What the Gospels themselves put on the far side of this no is the resurrection — and it is important to say carefully what that does and does not do to the case. It does not convert the no into a delayed yes: the cup was drunk, not removed; the prayer as asked was refused. What Easter does, within the Gospels' own claim, is vindicate the one who consented — the "not what I want" clause, not the "let it pass" clause, is the part of the prayer that the rest of the story honors. Christian tradition has therefore read Gethsemane as the model for praying under the possibility of refusal: ask plainly, repeat honestly, consent in advance, and walk toward whatever comes through the trees. The tradition has been far less willing to sit with the scene's other lesson — that a prayer can be perfect and still be denied.

The pattern, weighed

What the recorded denials have in common

Set the cases in a row and read across. Moses is refused the crossing and given the sight of the land, the successor, and a burial by God's own hand. David is refused the child's life and keeps the access that let him ask — his next act is worship. David is refused the temple and handed a dynasty. Elijah is refused death and given food, rest, and a commission. The man of Gerasa is refused the boat and handed the Decapolis. Paul is refused relief and told, uniquely, why — with grace named as the replacement. Jesus is refused the cup's passing and, on the Gospels' telling, vindicated through the very obedience the no made possible.

And the ledger runs longer than these seven. James and John ask for the seats at Jesus's right and left and are told those places are not his to appoint (Mark 10:35–40). A man in a crowd asks him to make his brother divide an inheritance, and gets back only a question about who appointed him judge (Luke 12:13–14). A whole generation weeps before the LORD at Kadesh and is not heeded (Deut 1:45). Jeremiah is told, three times, not to pray for his people at all (Jer 7:16; 11:14; 14:11), and when the elders come to consult Ezekiel, the word is "I will not be consulted by you" (Ezek 20:3) — the spoken edge of the silence the companion study calls judgment. None of those cases is worked here. They are cited for the reason this site keeps giving: so you can read them yourself.

Where this lands

Two observations, held at different strengths. The first is close to the text's surface and this study asserts it: in Scripture, a no to the faithful is an answer, not an ending — in every recorded case the relationship continues through the refusal, and in most of them the refused person's next recorded act is directed toward God, not away.

The second is a pattern and is held as one: the recorded denials do not arrive empty-handed — in six of the seven cases, something visible is set where the granted request would have stood: a view of the land, a kept access, a dynasty, bread and a commission, ten towns to tell, named grace. The seventh is the case this study calls the barest. What stands on the far side of Gethsemane's no is the resurrection, and that counts as the pattern's crowning instance only from inside Easter faith — from outside, the cell stays empty, and the pattern must be weighed at six. Whether the regularity reveals the character of God or the character of the canon — the stories Israel and the church chose to keep — is weighed honestly below, and readers building theology on this pattern should read that section as part of this one.

What no case supplies is the thing the sufferer most wants: the general explanation. Only Paul is told why, and what he is told is not a reason for the thorn but a resource against it. The denials of Scripture are answers without accountings — presence in place of explanation, all the way through.

Criticism

The strongest objections, left standing

The pattern may belong to the editors, not to God. This study finds that recorded denials come accompanied — a view, a dynasty, bread, a mission, grace. But the canon is a curated anthology, and consoling denials are precisely the kind a tradition preserves. The believer whose child died after seven days of fasting and who received no Jedidiah afterward left no chapter. If the sample is shaped by survivorship, then "the no never arrives empty-handed" is a fact about the stories that were kept, and turning it into an expectation — your loss will come with a replacement attached — repeats, gently, the error of Job's friends. The study's second landing claim is explicitly weakened to survive this objection; a reader may judge that it should have been dropped rather than weakened.

The pious responses may be coping, not communion. David rising to worship can be read as extraordinary faith or as the fatalism of a man skilled at moving on — the same king absorbs Absalom's death very differently. Paul's "I will boast all the more gladly" is written in a letter where he is defending his apostleship against rivals; a boast in weakness is, among other things, a rhetorically unanswerable move. The texts record behavior and speech; they do not open the heart for inspection. A psychological reading of every case in this study is available, and nothing in the texts refutes it — the choice between "he worshiped because he trusted" and "he worshiped because he had to do something" is made by the reader's frame, not the verse.

The hardest case is missing its explanation twice. The study leans on Moses as the paradigm denial, and Scripture's own accounts of why Moses was refused do not agree (Num 20:12 vs. Deut 3:26) — a seam flagged above and left open. But the objection cuts deeper than the flag admits: if the tradition could not settle the reason for its most prominent no, then the study's confidence that denials are meaningful answers rather than opaque outcomes rests substantially on the two New Testament cases where an interpretation is supplied (the thorn, the garden). Subtract those, and the Old Testament cases alone could as easily support a bleaker reading: that the no's of God are as unaccountable as the silences, and the difference between them is only that one of them speaks.

Conclusion

Praying under "who knows"

The set of three studies began with prayers that moved God, and honesty required the second panel — the cries that met silence. This panel completes the record: the prayers that were heard and refused. Read together, the three refuse a transaction theology from both directions. Faith is not a technique that produces yeses, and unanswered prayer is not a verdict on the one who prayed. The faithful of Scripture ask boldly, concede "who knows," and stay — through the yes, through the silence, and through the no.

Where the author lands, stated as confession: I believe the refusals in these texts were personal answers from a God who was present in them — that Moses on Nebo, David on the floor, and Paul with the thorn were dealt with, not merely declined. I hold that belief knowing the Criticism section names readings the texts cannot rule out, and knowing that the one case where the no's meaning is fully shown — the garden, read in the light of Easter — is visible as such only from inside the faith. What the texts themselves put beyond argument is smaller and still considerable: the Bible's tradition kept its refused prayers, named a Sabbath after one of them, and put its own Son's denial at the center of its longest night. Whatever else the divine no is in these pages, it is not something the text is ashamed of. The believer who has heard one is in the company the canon chose to keep.
