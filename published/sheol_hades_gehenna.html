The Words Behind "Hell"

The popular idea of hell fuses four vocabularies the texts keep apart — the words for sin, the word for harm, the words for the grave, and the words for judgment. This study opens the drawers one at a time.

Study
≈21 min read Challenging Word study Textual
Abstract

The popular concept of "hell" joins four sets of words that Scripture keeps distinct: the vocabulary of sin, the vocabulary of harm and calamity, the vocabulary of the realm of the dead, and the vocabulary of final judgment. This study sorts the four sets in their own texts, compares how the major translations handle each term, and then asks which connections between the sets the texts themselves state — and which arrived later.

Introduction

The chain, and the drawers

There is a chain most readers carry into the Bible before reading a verse of it. It runs: sin makes a person evil; evil people go to hell; hell is a fiery underworld where the devil reigns and the dead are punished. Every link feels biblical. The chain as a whole appears nowhere in the text — and the way to test that claim is not to argue about doctrine but to open the vocabulary and look.

Behind the English word "hell" the King James Version placed at least four different originals: the Hebrew realm of all the dead, its Greek-language equivalent, a burning valley outside Jerusalem's walls, and a one-verse prison for fallen angels. Behind the English word "evil," Hebrew has a word that covers calamity as readily as cruelty. Behind "sin," Hebrew keeps three distinct words with three distinct senses. Four vocabularies, each with its own texts and its own grammar — and the popular chain welds them into one substance. The method of this study is a word study's method: sort first, connect second. Each drawer is opened in its own texts; then, at the end, we ask which connections between the drawers the texts state in their own sentences.1

Where I start is worth stating, because this topic is contested and a reader deserves to know the author's lean before weighing his sorting. I come to this study doubting that the popular picture of hell is the Bible's picture, and the study exists because I wanted that doubt tested against the words rather than assumed. I write from the orthodox confession this site states openly. The sorting below is checkable — every count, rendering, and reference — whether or not a reader shares either the doubt or the confession.

Drawer one · Sin

Three Hebrew words, none of which means "evil"

The sin vocabulary is legal and relational. Its three principal words name three different ways of crossing a line that an authority has drawn:

sin, miss the mark (חָטָא | ḥāṭāʾ | Heb - H2398) is the broadest and most common. Its concrete sense survives in Judges 20:16, where seven hundred left-handed slingers "could sling a stone at a hair and not miss" — the same verb. Failure against a standard, not necessarily willful. transgression, rebellion (פֶּשַׁע | pešaʿ | Heb - H6588) is breach against a sovereign; the political use is on the surface of 2 Kings 1:1, where "Moab rebelled against Israel" uses the verb of the same root (pāšaʿ, H6586) as Israel's rebellion against God. iniquity (עָוֺן | ʿāwōn | Heb - H5771) is twistedness with its guilt and consequence attached — Cain's "my punishment is greater than I can bear" (Gen 4:13) uses it, fusing deed, guilt, and outcome in one word. A fourth word behaves differently from all three: wicked (רָשָׁע | rāšāʿ | Heb - H7563) is a characterization, not an act — the party a court rightly condemns (Deut 25:1), the settled "way" of Psalm 1 — and Ezekiel insists the wicked can turn and live (Ezek 18:21, 27), which means the word names an orientation, not a fate.2

Two narratives show what the sin vocabulary tracks, because in both of them a person sins while doing something useful. Uzzah reaches out to steady the ark when the oxen stumble — a protective act — and dies for touching what could not be touched (2 Sam 6:6–7). King Uzziah enters the temple to burn incense — an act of worship — and is struck with a skin disease for taking a priest's office (2 Chr 26:16–21). No one was harmed by Uzzah's hand or Uzziah's censer. The two scenes illustrate what the lexical data above already established: sin tracks the boundary set by the operative authority, not damage done. The distinction rests on the word-definitions; the narratives just make it visible.

A caution the Greek imposes

The Septuagint, translating these words into Greek two centuries and more before Jesus, pulled most of the sin vocabulary into sin (ἁμαρτία | hamartia | Gk - G266) and its relatives — flattening in Greek the distinctions Hebrew keeps. The New Testament inherits that flattened vocabulary. Any argument built on the Hebrew sorting has to reckon with this: the apostles wrote in the language that had already merged the drawers' labels, even where their thought kept the contents apart. (A full concordance survey of every LXX rendering of the Hebrew sin-words remains owed; the flattening claim rests on the general lexical fact that Greek hamartia and its family absorb most of the Hebrew range, not on an exhaustive tally.)

Drawer two · Harm

Raʿ — the word English hardened

The second drawer holds one word with a wide range. evil, harm, calamity (רַע | raʿ | Heb - H7451) covers harm suffered as readily as harm done. The same word describes the wickedness of humankind before the flood (Gen 6:5) and the calamity God himself announces: "I make weal and create woe (raʿ)" (Isa 45:7); "Does disaster (raʿ) befall a city, unless the LORD has done it?" (Amos 3:6). English "evil" has hardened almost entirely onto the moral and sinister end of that range. Hebrew never did. Context, not the lexicon, decides which sense is live in a given verse.

Set the first two drawers side by side and they refuse to merge. God does raʿ-as-calamity without sinning — the sin vocabulary is not, so far as this study finds, applied to him anywhere in the Hebrew Bible. Uzzah sins without doing raʿ — no good was destroyed by his steadying hand. The popular chain's first link, sin makes a person evil, fuses a legal category with a harm category that the Hebrew Bible keeps in separate drawers and even runs in opposite directions. Where the two meet is instructive: the recurring formula "the evil in the eyes of the LORD" (used as the standard verdict on a king's or a generation's conduct roughly forty times across Judges through Chronicles — e.g. Judg 2:11; 1 Kgs 11:6; 2 Kgs 21:2 — count via topical concordance survey, not an independent word-by-word tally) names moral evil relationally — conduct God evaluates as destructive — which is a judgment rendered at the meeting point of the drawers, not an identity between them.3

Drawer three · The dead

Sheol and Hades — the realm everyone enters

Sheol (שְׁאוֹל | šəʾôl | Heb - H7585) occurs about sixty-five times in the Hebrew Bible,4 and the most consequential fact about it is who goes there: everyone. It is not a punishment address. Jacob, the covenant patriarch, expects it as a matter of course — "I shall go down to Sheol to my son, mourning" (Gen 37:35) — and Hannah's prayer states its theology in one line:

"The LORD kills and brings to life; he brings down to Sheol and raises up."

1 Samuel 2:6 · NRSVue

Sheol is below (people go down to it), dark, and quiet. Job describes it as the great leveler — kings, counselors, prisoners, and slaves together (Job 3:13–19). Its dominant notes are silence and cessation: "there is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in Sheol" (Eccl 9:10); "the dead do not praise the LORD" (Ps 115:17). And it sits entirely inside God's reach — "if I make my bed in Sheol, you are there" (Ps 139:8; cf. Amos 9:2). Whatever Sheol is, it is not a rival kingdom, and dying into it is not, in the ordinary run of the text, a verdict — the wicked and the righteous alike lie down in it as a simple fact of mortality. One narrative complicates the "ordinary run" honestly: Korah's company is swallowed alive into Sheol as the enacted sentence for rebellion (Num 16:30–33) — a real exception, not a data point to smooth over. What the exception does not do is turn Sheol itself into a punishment address: the earth opening under Korah is a singular sign-act the text marks as unprecedented ("if the LORD creates something new," 16:30), not evidence that Sheol's ordinary character is penal. The rule and its one clear exception both belong on the page. Two Hebrew words travel with it in poetry without adding territory — the pit (bôr, H953; Ps 88:4–6) and the pit of decay (šaḥat, H7845; Isa 38:17) — and a sixth word, Abaddon, destruction (אֲבַדּוֹן | ʾăḇaddôn | Heb - H11), sits beside Sheol in the Wisdom books as poetry's personified Destruction (Job 26:6; 28:22; 31:12; Ps 88:11; Prov 15:11; 27:20) — six occurrences on the count that includes Prov 27:20's variant spelling, five on a stricter count (the footnote gives the textual reason for the split) — twice given a half-voice the way Job's poetry also lets the Deep and the Sea speak (Job 28:14, 22). Translations split on how to carry the word over: NIV renders it by sense ("Destruction"), where ESV and NRSVue transliterate the name "Abaddon" — the same Hebrew underneath, handled by two different translation philosophies.5

The author's reading — and the spectrum it sits on

My own lean, stated as a lean: read strictly in its own texts, Sheol looks less like a mapped netherworld than like death itself spoken concretely — the ground, the grave, the condition of being dead, expressed in the most natural and sometimes poetic way available to the language. Neutral, non-punitive, and near. Scholarship runs a spectrum on this. At one end, the reading that Sheol is simply the grave (R. Laird Harris, arguing from parallels in Hebrew poetic texts, in the Theological Wordbook of the Old Testament and elsewhere6); in the middle, Philip Johnston's Shades of Sheol — which argues the Hebrew Bible does not present Sheol as everyone's flat, neutral destiny: the word attaches overwhelmingly to death under judgment or distress, and the tidy division of Sheol into compartments for the righteous and the wicked is a Second Temple development that "the Old Testament texts do not assert";7 further out, a fuller mythological underworld read against the ancient Near Eastern background. My lean sits nearest the first position, and Johnston's is the reading that presses back hardest against it. The "gates" and "bars" imagery (Isa 38:10; Job 17:16), and Korah's company — named above as the one clear penal exception — are the data the middle position presses hardest, and the reader should know both are live.

What the narratives add — four ways the dead return

The poetry says the dead are silent. The narratives are not so uniform, and a study that sorts vocabulary owes the same sorting to the ways Scripture lets death open. There are four, and they are not one thing:8

The hardest of the four for any flat reading of Sheol is the first. At En-dor, in prose narrative, a medium brings up Samuel; the text calls him Samuel without qualification; he complains of being disturbed, prophesies, and the prophecy comes true (1 Sam 28:15–19). The laws against necromancy (Deut 18:11; Isa 8:19) legislate against a practice the narrative treats as capable of working. Whether En-dor reports a folk practice the text refuses to endorse, or a one-time act God permitted, the scene stands in the canon, and this study does not explain it away. What the four-way sorting shows is the direction of the whole shelf: the Old Testament's categories for death opening are occasional and exceptional until its latest text states the general one — "many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt" (Dan 12:2). Resurrection is not an import into this vocabulary; it is the last drawer of the death shelf, opened once, at the canon's late edge.

Hades — the same shelf in Greek

When the New Testament needs Sheol in Greek it says Hades (ᾅδης | hadēs | Gk - G86), and it says so on the record: Peter at Pentecost quotes Psalm 16:10 with Hades standing where the Hebrew had Sheol, and builds the resurrection argument on it (Acts 2:27–31). Between the Testaments the concept had developed — Second Temple literature already pictures a compartmented interim (1 Enoch 22) — and the New Testament carries three claims about it. Hades is locked, and the risen Christ holds its keys (Rev 1:18; cf. Matt 16:18's failing gates). Hades is temporary by explicit statement: it gives up its dead and is itself thrown into the lake of fire (Rev 20:13–14) — which makes any equation of Hades with a final "hell" a category mistake by Revelation's own accounting. And one passage furnishes its interior, whose genre divides readers sharply enough to need its own box.9

Luke 16 — cosmology or parable?

The rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31) supplies nearly everything the popular imagination knows about the intermediate state: comfort at Abraham's side, torment in Hades, the fixed chasm, conversation across it. One reading takes the scene as information about the interim dead. The other takes it as a parable told in the furniture of its day — compartmented Sheol was already in circulation (1 Enoch 22), reversal-after-death tales were told more widely, and the story's stated punch lands on wealth, indifference, and the line "neither will they be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" (16:31). The trouble with settling it: the story opens like Luke's parables and behaves like one (the only parable with a named character), yet Jesus nowhere flags its scenery as fiction, and the scenery agrees with other interim hints (Rev 6:9–11; Phil 1:23). This study builds its map from passages whose genre is not in question and treats Luke 16 as corroboration at most. Readers who weight it more heavily are doing something defensible that this page declines to do.

Drawer four · Judgment

Gehenna — a valley with a history, and the terms beyond it

Gehenna (γέεννα | geenna | Gk - G1067) is not a synonym for Hades, and the difference begins with geography. The word is Greek for the Hebrew Gê-Hinnōm, the Valley of Hinnom — a real ravine south and southwest of Jerusalem's walls that a first-century hearer could walk to. Its reputation was already black: there Ahaz and Manasseh burned their own sons (2 Chr 28:3; 33:6), and there Jeremiah stood and renamed the ground "the valley of Slaughter" (Jer 7:31–32; 19:6). Jeremiah's Topheth oracle deserves to be read whole, because it contains more of the valley's future than the popular memory keeps. In the same oracle, spoken in the valley itself, the LORD indicts the child-burning (19:5) and then announces the siege that will follow: "I will make them eat the flesh of their sons and the flesh of their daughters" (19:9) — cannibalism inside the starving city, the very horror Lamentations later records (Lam 4:10). The valley's own oracle binds together idolatrous bloodshed, national catastrophe, and unburied dead. That is the loaded landmark Jesus points at when he says Gehenna.10

The word occurs twelve times in the New Testament — eleven on Jesus' lips, once from James, never from Paul.11 Two texts carry most of the doctrinal weight. The first marks Gehenna off from everything in drawer three:

"Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul; rather, fear the one who can destroy both soul and body in hell."

Matthew 10:28 · NRSVue (Gk: Gehenna)

Both soul and body: the verse does not itself state a chronology, but the most natural reading is that Gehenna belongs after resurrection, when body and soul are rejoined — which is what would distinguish it from Hades, the realm of the dead awaiting that rejoining; the inference rests on this verse's pairing of the two, not on a stated timeline elsewhere. And the one to fear there is God (Luke 12:5). Nothing in the text seats Satan as warden of Gehenna; Scripture's devil is one of the judged, never the jailer. The second text is the triple warning of Mark 9:43–48 — hand, foot, eye; better maimed into life than whole into Gehenna, "where their worm never dies and the fire is never quenched." The closing line is Isaiah 66:24, the last verse of that book, where the redeemed look on corpses — an image, at its source, of the dead being consumed, not of chambers below.

What did Jesus's hearers hear? — the referent question

By the first century, Gehenna was already stock Jewish shorthand for final judgment: 1 Enoch 27 places "this accursed valley" as the gathering place of the condemned in "the last days," and 4 Ezra 7:36 — written after Jesus but witnessing the same development — sets "the furnace of Gehenna" opposite "the Paradise of delight."12 The majority reading therefore takes Jesus's Gehenna sayings as eschatological: final judgment, pictured in the cursed valley. A minority reading with serious scholarly backing — N. T. Wright, in Jesus and the Victory of God, is characterized in the secondary literature as arguing the Gehenna sayings have more to do with the coming destruction of Jerusalem than with a post-mortem state, though this page has not yet pulled the primary-text page citation12 — hears a this-worldly first horizon: warnings about the national catastrophe bearing down on the city — the road that ends, as Jeremiah's oracle ended, in siege, slaughter, and the valley full of the dead, which is where AD 70 in fact ended. A third position holds both horizons at once: judgment language whose near edge is historical and whose far edge is final. The choice changes what several sayings mean, and this study does not make it; the drawer's contents — a real valley, a prophetic curse, a Second Temple symbol, twelve sayings — are what every position must work from.

A correction worth keeping — the garbage dump

The often-repeated claim that Gehenna was Jerusalem's perpetually burning garbage dump, its fires never going out, has no ancient support. Its earliest known source is Rabbi David Kimhi's commentary on Psalm 27, around AD 1200 — a millennium too late to inform Jesus's usage — and no archaeological or first-century literary evidence backs it; the correction is documented in Lloyd R. Bailey, "Gehenna: The Topography of Hell," Biblical Archaeologist 49/3 (1986). The valley's first-century associations were the ones the prophets gave it: child sacrifice, slaughter, and unburied dead. The dump image is repeated in sermons and study notes to this day, which makes it a live specimen of how a picture can attach itself to a word and travel farther than the texts underneath it.

The prisons that hold no humans, and the terms at the map's ends

Two further terms complete the "hell" vocabulary, and neither contains a single human being. Tartaros appears once, as a verb: God "cast them into Tartaros" — the angels who sinned, held "in chains of deepest darkness... until the judgment" (2 Pet 2:4; tartarōsas, G5020). The Septuagint had already used the name — Job 40:20 reads en tō tartarō ("in Tartaros"), Job 41:24 reads "the Tartaros of the abyss," and Prov 30:16 adds tartaros as its own term to the Hebrew list of the insatiable (verified against the Rahlfs LXX) — so Peter is borrowing the deepest dungeon Greek had — for rebel powers, not the human dead. The abyss (abyssos, G12) is the working prison of demonic powers in the present age: Legion begs not to be sent there (Luke 8:31); its shaft opens at the fifth trumpet (Rev 9:1–2); Satan is bound in it for the thousand years (Rev 20:1–3). Over its opened shaft Revelation places a king whose name closes a trail from drawer three:

"They have as king over them the angel of the bottomless pit; his name in Hebrew is Abaddon, and in Greek he is called Apollyon."

Revelation 9:11 · NRSVue

Whether John intends a real angelic being, a personification of destruction, or a satirical shadow of the destroying empire of his day is argued — the Greek name Apollyōn may glance at Apollo, the god the emperors claimed — and the options are weighed in the scholarship section below rather than settled here.13 The lake of fire is Revelation's term and Revelation's only: never called Gehenna, Hades, or Sheol in the text. Its sequence is precise — beast and false prophet (Rev 19:20), the devil (20:10, with "tormented day and night forever and ever" said in that verse of those three), then Death and Hades themselves (20:14), then "anyone whose name was not found written in the book of life" (20:15). Twice the text glosses itself: "This is the second death" (20:14; 21:8). Paradise is the map's other pole — the dying thief's "today you will be with me in Paradise" (Luke 23:43), Paul caught up into it (2 Cor 12:3–4), the tree of life within it (Rev 2:7) — the interim of the righteous: with Christ, comforted, and still waiting for resurrection (Phil 1:23 with 1 Thess 4:13–16).

The joints

What the texts connect — and what they never did

Sorting is half the study. The other half is owed to the popular chain, because the chain did not come from nowhere: some of its links are in the text. The difference between a joint and a weld is the difference between two boards fastened where they meet and two boards fused into one. The texts fasten the drawers at specific points. They never fuse them.

The joints the texts state. Jesus welds the sin vocabulary and Gehenna grammatically, in one sentence, three times over: "If your hand causes you to stumble, cut it off; it is better for you to enter life maimed than to have two hands and to go to hell [Gehenna]" (Mark 9:43, NRSVue verified; cf. 9:45, 47; Matt 5:29–30; 18:8–9). The verb is skandalizō, "cause to stumble" — a near neighbor of sin rather than the word itself (NRSVue's choice, over KJV's older "offend," is the more literal rendering), but a stumble that costs a hand is not a neutral trip: the passage is naming sin's occasion, and Matthew's parallel keeps the same structure. Revelation joins deeds and the lake: the dead are "judged according to their works," and the vice list of 21:8 ends in the second death. Paul joins sin and death itself: "the wages of sin is death" (Rom 6:23). Whoever says Scripture never connects sin to judgment has not read these sentences; they are the chain's genuine anchors, and this study states them at full strength.

The welds the texts never made. Sin is not raʿ: the vocabularies run on different axes, and each occurs where the other cannot follow (drawers one and two). Sheol is not punishment: everyone enters it, patriarchs included, and no one is sent there as a verdict (drawer three). Hades is not the final fire: it is emptied into it (Rev 20:13–14). Satan does not rule any of these places: he fears the abyss (Luke 8:31), is bound in it (Rev 20:2–3), and ends in the lake (20:10); the only authority over Gehenna the Gospels name is God's (Luke 12:5). And nowhere does any text call the realm of the dead and the place of judgment by one name — that unification is the work of a single English word, "hell," applied by translators to four originals.

The chain's error, sorted this way, is not that it connects the drawers. It is that it replaces the stated connections with identities: sin equals evil, death equals punishment, the grave equals the fire, and the devil owns the whole. Each equation erases a distinction the texts spend real ink maintaining. The connections are joints — judgment falls on deeds; sin, unrepented, walks toward the fire — and a joint, unlike a weld, lets you see where one board ends and the other begins.

The scholarship

Where the scholars agree, and where no one should pretend to

Named positions in this section were checked against each scholar's published work or reliable secondary literature. Four are carried as verified direct quotations — Peterson, Stott, Hart, and Johnston (notes 15–17, 7); the rest are presented as fair characterizations of the field, with a direct quotation still owed.14

Development across the canon

The mainstream account of Sheol is developmental. Philip Johnston's Shades of Sheol reads the Old Testament as presenting not one fixed doctrine but a picture that thickens — neutral realm in the early material, moral differentiation in the prophets' poetry, two-directional resurrection arriving explicitly at Daniel 12. John Walton adds the caution from genre: early texts' silence about post-mortem differentiation shows what those genres needed to say, not everything early Israel believed. Between the Testaments the development accelerates on any account — 1 Enoch's compartments and Qumran's eschatology show Judaism holding differentiated pictures of the interim dead before Jesus told a story about a rich man and a beggar. N. T. Wright's larger argument frames the shelf: what distinguished Jewish hope was never a doctrine of the underworld but the expectation of bodily resurrection out of it. The four-way sorting above (apparition, resuscitation, translation, resurrection) bears on this development story in one way worth naming: the raw materials of the fourth category are present in the earliest strata — dead who persist, dead who return, the living who do not die — so the late arrival of resurrection doctrine reads as growth out of the shelf's own contents rather than as a foreign import.

The Gehenna debate — held open, on the record

What Gehenna's fire finally does to those in it is the most contested question in biblical eschatology, and this site's standing rule is that genuinely open questions are surveyed, not settled. Three positions, each with real textual footing:

Eternal conscious torment
The majority tradition of Western Christianity since Augustine. Leans on Rev 20:10's "tormented day and night forever and ever," Matt 25:46's symmetry (the same aiōnios for punishment and life), and the theological argument that guilt against the infinite bears infinite consequence. Robert Peterson reads Isaiah 66:24's source image as pointing past destruction: its "undying worms and inextinguishable fire" point "to the final doom of the wicked — eternal punishment."15
Conditional immortality / annihilation
The wicked are finally destroyed, not endlessly tormented. Leans on Matt 10:28's apollymi ("destroy both soul and body"), Isaiah 66:24's source-image of corpses, and "second death" read as death. Argued at length in Edward Fudge's The Fire That Consumes; John Stott advanced it only tentatively, and candidly: "I find the concept [of eternal conscious torment] intolerable and do not understand how people can live with it without either cauterising their feelings or cracking under the strain."16
Universal reconciliation
All are ultimately restored. Leans on the cosmic-scope texts (1 Cor 15:28; Phil 2:10–11; Col 1:20) and reads aiōnios as "of the age" rather than endless. Von Balthasar held it only as a hope; David Bentley Hart presses it as a claim — that "the universalist understanding of [Christianity's] message is the only one possible," said "without the least hesitation or qualification."17 Its standing objection is Matt 25:46's symmetry.

Each position can cite verses the others must work to accommodate, and the choice among them turns on judgment calls — how much weight Revelation's imagery can carry, what aiōnios quantifies, whether Isaiah's corpses set the meaning of Jesus's fire — that the texts themselves do not adjudicate. The summary table above marks Gehenna and the lake of fire "final" without specifying the experiential content of that finality. That is as far as the method goes.

The descent — "he went and made proclamation to the spirits in prison"

First Peter 3:18–20 (with Eph 4:8–10 alongside) stands behind the creed's "he descended." Four readings persist: the patristic descent-to-Hades with proclamation to the dead; the "descent" as the incarnation itself; Christ preaching through Noah, in Noah's day, by the Spirit; and William Dalton's — now widely followed in critical commentary — that the risen Christ proclaimed victory, not an offer, to imprisoned angelic spirits. The leading reading routes through the same 2 Peter 2:4 / Jude 6 territory as Tartaros above: the prison passages hang together, and they are about powers, not people.

A text-critical note on the undying worm

In the KJV, the worm-and-fire refrain appears three times in Mark 9 (vv. 44, 46, 48). In the earliest manuscripts it appears once, at verse 48; verses 44 and 46 are absent from the oldest witnesses and are judged by modern critical editions to be early copyists' amplifications, which is why modern translations skip those verse numbers. Nothing doctrinal hangs on the difference — verse 48 says what the two echoes said — but a reader comparing Bibles deserves to know why the verses "disappear." (Confirmed against the standard critical editions: NA28/UBS omit vv. 44 and 46; the worm-and-fire refrain is text-critically original only at v. 48.)

Where this lands

"Hell" is an English filing decision, not a biblical word — and the popular chain behind it fuses four vocabularies the texts keep sorted. Sin is a legal category and can occur without harm; raʿ is a harm category and can occur without sin. The realm of the dead holds everyone, punishes no one, and is destined to be emptied. Final judgment lies on the far side of resurrection, in a vocabulary of its own, with its inner nature stated in images the traditions have never agreed how to read.

The texts do connect the drawers — sin to judgment, deeds to the fire, death to the wages of sin — but as joints, not identities. The sorting is this study's claim. The contested question of what the final fire does stays contested here, by the site's own rule.

Criticism

The strongest objections, left standing

Distinct vocabulary does not prove disconnected reality. A systematic theologian can grant every word study on this page and still build an integrated doctrine of hell from the joints the page itself concedes: if sin leads to judgment and judgment to the fire, then the chain — as a summary of where the road goes, rather than a lexicon entry — may be doing its job. Canons speak of one reality from many angles; four vocabularies could be four camera positions on a single subject. The study's answer, that the popular chain asserts identities rather than connections, carries weight only if readers actually hold the identities — and a defender of the tradition may fairly reply that the best theologians always distinguished what the popular imagination fused.

The welds are older than the medieval imagination. This page's sorting could leave the impression that fusing the drawers was a late corruption. But Second Temple Judaism had already connected sin, death, and fiery judgment into integrated pictures before Jesus spoke — 1 Enoch's compartments sort the dead morally in the interim, not just at the end — and Jesus taught inside that Judaism without pausing to correct its map. If the welds were fabrications, they were available fabrications in the first century, and the Gospels' silence about them is data a deconstruction has to carry, not dismiss.

The tidy sequence is a construction. No biblical author lays out "Sheol → resurrection → Gehenna → lake of fire" as a single scheme; this study assembled it from texts spanning a thousand years, multiple genres, and two languages. A reader who thinks Revelation's imagery was never meant as cosmological sequencing — that "Death and Hades thrown into the fire" is a poet's way of saying death dies, not a disposal schedule — loses nothing from the individual word studies and owes this page's overall map no allegiance.

Word-counting is not theology. The method leans on occurrence tallies and lexical sorting — sixty-five Sheols, twelve Gehennas, one Tartaros. But meaning does not distribute evenly across occurrences, and a term's theology can be carried by texts that never use it: the parable of the weeds burns what it burns without naming Gehenna, and the outer darkness has no entry in this study's table at all. The vocabulary-first frame corrects a real error — the KJV's collapse — but it risks the inverse error of treating the map of words as the map of the thing.

Conclusion

Reading the words before the doctrine

The English word "hell" has been asked to do the work of at least four originals, and the originals refuse to be one thing. Sorting them does not decide the hardest question — it locates it. The debate that matters is not whether Sheol equals Gehenna (it does not; that much is vocabulary), but what the final judgment's fire is and does — and there the texts hand every party imagery and deny every party a diagram.

Where I land, held as confession: I take the sequence the sorted words yield — interim, resurrection, judgment, permanence — as the Bible's own frame, and I decline to fill in the permanence beyond what is written, because the writers themselves declined. Every term for death's custody is temporary, every lock has a keyholder, and the only voice in the New Testament that holds "the keys of Death and of Hades" spent three days on the wrong side of the door to get them. The map's contested edges are real, and they stay open on this site. Its center — that death's realm is emptied by resurrection — is not where the ambiguity lives.

A closing comment from the author — opinion, not finding

The findings above stand on their own; this box is where I say what I make of them, and it should be weighed accordingly. Three facts from the study sit with me. Everyone in the Old Testament dies into the same place, and it is not a torture chamber. The only person the Gospels authorize to judge anyone into Gehenna is God — not the devil, and not me. And the sayings of Jesus that use the word point at a valley whose own prophet had already named what happens to a people who burn their children and will not turn: the siege comes, and the valley fills. I read the warnings first the way I would have heard them standing there — look what became of them; do not become it — while granting, as the study's own referent box says, that more may be in view. What I refuse to carry away is the chain: the picture where death itself is punishment, the grave is fire, and the devil runs it. The words will not support it, and fear built on words the text did not say is not reverence. What comes after judgment, in its inner nature, I leave where the writers left it. I hold the confession this site states, I trust the keyholder, and I decline to draw the parts of the map he did not.
