The Words Behind "Hell"

Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, Tartaros, the abyss, and Abaddon — six different words, one English label, and what each one actually names

Study
≈17 min read Challenging Word study Textual
Abstract

Older English Bibles render several distinct Hebrew and Greek words with the single word "hell." This study takes the words one at a time — Sheol and the pit, Abaddon, Hades, Gehenna, Tartaros, the abyss, the lake of fire, and paradise — tracing what each names in its own texts, comparing how the major translations handle each, and then surveying where scholarship agrees and where the meaning of the hardest term remains genuinely contested.

The problem

One English word, six originals

Open a King James Bible to Psalm 16:10 and the psalmist's soul is in hell. Turn to Matthew 16:18 and the gates of hell shall not prevail. Turn to Mark 9:43 and it is better to enter life maimed than to go into hell. Three passages, one English word — and three different words in the original languages, naming three different things: the Hebrew realm of all the dead, its Greek-language equivalent, and a burning valley outside Jerusalem that Jesus made the image of final judgment. Add the one-verse prison of fallen angels in 2 Peter, and the KJV's single word "hell" is covering four originals; add the abyss and the personified Destruction the Old Testament calls Abaddon, and the conceptual territory holds six distinct terms before the lake of fire and paradise complete the map.

This matters beyond pedantry, because the words do different work. One of them holds everyone who dies, righteous and wicked alike. One of them holds no humans at all. One is temporary by explicit statement; one is called the second death. A doctrine of "hell" assembled without sorting the vocabulary is a doctrine of a word no biblical author used. The method of this study is therefore a word study's method: each term is taken in its own texts first, and the translations are compared where the rendering is itself the question.1

One introductory precision: modern translations have not uniformly retired "hell." The NRSVue prints Sheol and Hades as names, but still renders Gehenna as "hell" (with a footnote), and handles 2 Peter's Tartaros with "hell" and a note as well.2 The collapse survives in softened form even in the best modern versions — which is a reason to do this study, not a reason it cannot be done.

Part one · The Hebrew shelf

Sheol — the realm everyone enters

Sheol (שְׁאוֹל | šəʾôl | Heb - H7585) occurs about sixty-five times in the Hebrew Bible,3 and on the oldest and broadest reading its most load-bearing feature is who goes there: everyone, righteous and wicked alike — not, on this reading, a punishment address but the common destiny of the dead. Jacob, the covenant patriarch, expects it as a matter of course — "I shall go down to Sheol to my son, mourning" (Gen 37:35) — and the psalmist treats escape from it as a human impossibility: "Who can live and never see death? Who can escape the power of Sheol?" (Ps 89:48). Hannah's prayer states the theology in one line:

"The LORD kills and brings to life; he brings down to Sheol and raises up."

1 Samuel 2:6 · NRSVue

Sheol is below (people go down to it), dark, and quiet. Job describes it as the great leveler — kings, counselors, prisoners, and slaves together, "the small and the great are there" (Job 3:13–19). Its dominant notes are silence and cessation: "there is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in Sheol" (Eccl 9:10); "the dead do not praise the LORD, nor do any that go down into silence" (Ps 115:17). And it sits entirely inside God's jurisdiction — "if I make my bed in Sheol, you are there" (Ps 139:8); "though they dig into Sheol, from there shall my hand take them" (Amos 9:2). Whatever Sheol is, it is not a rival kingdom.

That flat "everyone," though, is exactly where the leading modern study pushes back: Philip Johnston argues the word Sheol in fact clusters around death under judgment, with the righteous shown descending to it only in extremity — so the universal-grave reading may over-read the specific term. The qualification is taken up in the scholarship section; it tempers the claim without dissolving its core, since even on Johnston's reading Sheol names death's realm, not a place of final punishment. This study keeps the broad reading as its working frame and marks the seam rather than hiding it.

The edges of the picture

Three groups of texts complicate the flat portrait, and they are the seeds of everything that develops later. Two prophetic taunt-songs give Sheol furniture and voices: dead kings rise from their thrones to greet the fallen tyrant of Babylon — "Are you too become as weak as we?" (Isa 14:9–15) — and Ezekiel arranges the fallen nations in the pit by their conduct, with "the uncircumcised, slain by the sword" in their assigned places (Ezek 32:21–24). Both passages are high poetry with a political point, and pressing their imagery into cosmology is risky; what they show at minimum is that Israel could imagine the dead as present, aware, and differently placed. Alongside them, Psalm 49 draws the first clear line of different outcomes: the foolish are "appointed for Sheol; Death shall be their shepherd… But God will ransom my soul from the power of Sheol, for he will receive me" (Ps 49:14–15). And one prophetic text points past Sheol altogether — the verse Paul will one day fire at death like an arrow:

"Shall I ransom them from the power of Sheol? Shall I redeem them from Death? O Death, where are your plagues? O Sheol, where is your destruction?"

Hosea 13:14 · NRSVue

The Old Testament's last word on the subject is its latest: "Many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt" (Dan 12:2). Two-directional resurrection, stated once, at the canon's late edge. The shelf closes with Sheol still holding its dead — and with notice given that it will not hold them forever.

The pit — Sheol's poetic doubles

Two Hebrew words travel with Sheol in the poetry without adding new territory: pit, cistern (בּוֹר | bôr | Heb - H953), which leans on depth and imprisonment — "I am counted among those who go down to the Pit… in the regions dark and deep" (Ps 88:4–6) — and pit of destruction (שַׁחַת | šaḥat | Heb - H7845), which leans on decay — Hezekiah's "you have held back my life from the pit of destruction" (Isa 38:17). Jonah uses Sheol and the pit for the same experience in the same prayer (Jonah 2:2, 6). They are intensifiers of Sheol, not additions to the map.

Abaddon — destruction with a capital letter

The sixth word behaves unlike the others: it is barely a place at all. Abaddon, (place of) destruction (אֲבַדּוֹן | ʾăḇaddôn | Heb - H11), from the root ʾāḇad ("to perish"), appears six times in the Hebrew Bible, always in poetry and always in company — paired with Sheol, Death, or the grave, like a second name on the same door:4

Twice in Job the word does something the other underworld terms never do this early: it half-speaks. Abaddon "has no covering" before God; Abaddon and Death "say, 'We have heard a rumor of it.'" This is personification inside Hebrew poetry — Destruction as a character — and it is worth holding onto, because the New Testament will finish the thought. Translation handling differs by philosophy rather than text: the NIV renders the word by sense ("Destruction"), where ESV and NRSVue transliterate it as the name "Abaddon" — the same Hebrew underneath.5

Part two · The Greek shelf

Hades — Sheol in Greek dress, developed

When the New Testament needs Sheol in Greek, it says Hades (ᾅδης | hadēs | Gk - G86), and it says so on the record: Peter at Pentecost quotes Psalm 16:10 with Hades standing exactly where the Hebrew had Sheol, and builds the resurrection argument on it — David "was not abandoned to Hades," but Jesus was raised (Acts 2:27–31). The equivalence is the apostles' own. The word occurs ten times in the modern critical text; the count behind the KJV is eleven, because its Greek text also read "Hades" in Paul's taunt at 1 Corinthians 15:55 ("O grave, where is thy victory?"), where the older manuscripts read "death" twice — one of the small places where the translation question and the manuscript question touch.6

By the first century, the concept has developed past the quiet Sheol of Job. Three New Testament claims carry the development:

Hades is locked, and Christ holds the key. "I was dead, and see, I am alive forever and ever; and I have the keys of Death and of Hades" (Rev 1:18). The image assumes a realm that holds its dead against their will — and announces that its lock has changed hands. The same image stands behind "the gates of Hades will not prevail" (Matt 16:18): gates are what a stronghold defends itself with; death's power to keep what it takes is the thing that fails.

Hades is temporary by explicit statement. "Death and Hades gave up the dead that were in them, and all were judged according to what they had done. Then Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire" (Rev 20:13–14). Whatever else is debated about last things, this is on the page: Hades empties, and Hades ends. It is a holding realm with a demolition date, which makes any equation of Hades with a final "hell" a category mistake by Revelation's own accounting.

One passage furnishes its interior — and its genre is disputed. The rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31) supplies nearly everything the popular imagination knows about the intermediate state: the poor man carried to Abraham's side, the rich man "in Hades, where he was being tormented," the flame and the thirst, the great chasm fixed between, the conversation across it. The body asserts nothing from this passage beyond what it visibly contains; what it contains, and what it may be for, divide readers sharply enough that the question gets its own box.

Luke 16 — cosmology or parable?

One reading takes the scene as information: Jesus describes the real geography of the intermediate state — division, consciousness, comfort and torment, an impassable gulf. The other reading takes it as a parable told in the furniture of its day: Jewish popular imagination already held versions of this scene (compartmented Sheol appears in 1 Enoch 22, and reversal-after-death tales circulated more widely), and the story's stated punch lands elsewhere — on wealth, on indifference, and on the line "neither will they be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" (16:31). The trouble with settling it: the story is introduced like Luke's parables and behaves like one (it is the only one with a named character), yet Jesus nowhere flags its scenery as fiction, and the scenery agrees with other NT hints (Rev 6:9–11; Phil 1:23). This study builds its map from the passages whose genre is not in question and treats Luke 16 as corroboration at most. Readers who weight it more heavily are doing something defensible that this page declines to do.

Part three · The valley

Gehenna — a place on the map before it was a doctrine

Gehenna (γέεννα | geenna | Gk - G1067) is not a synonym for Hades, and the difference begins with geography. The word is Greek for the Hebrew Gê-Hinnōm, the Valley of Hinnom, a real ravine south and southwest of Jerusalem's walls that a first-century hearer could walk to. Its reputation was already black with history: there Ahaz and Manasseh burned their own sons as offerings (2 Chr 28:3; 33:6), and there Jeremiah stood and renamed the ground — "no longer the Topheth, or the valley of the son of Hinnom, but the valley of Slaughter" (Jer 7:31–32; 19:6). By the Second Temple period the valley's name had become Jewish shorthand for final judgment. When Jesus says Gehenna, he is not coining a term; he is pointing at a landmark his hearers' prophets had already cursed.

The word occurs twelve times in the New Testament — eleven on Jesus' lips, once from James, and never from Paul.7 Two of Jesus' uses carry most of the doctrinal weight, and they mark Gehenna off from everything in the previous sections:

"Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul; rather, fear the one who can destroy both soul and body in hell."

Matthew 10:28 · NRSVue (Gk: Gehenna)

Both soul and body: Gehenna belongs after resurrection, when body and soul are rejoined — which is exactly what distinguishes it from Hades, the realm of the dead awaiting that rejoining. And the agent is God — "fear the one who… has authority to cast into hell" (Luke 12:5). The verse quietly deletes a millennium of art: nothing in the text seats Satan as the warden of Gehenna; Scripture's devil is one of the judged there, never the jailer.

The second weight-bearing text is the triple warning of Mark 9:43–48 — hand, foot, eye; better maimed into life than whole into Gehenna, "where their worm never dies and the fire is never quenched." That closing line is Isaiah's, and the borrowing matters: Isaiah 66:24, the last verse of the book, where the redeemed look on "the dead bodies of the people who have rebelled against me," their worm undying, their fire unquenched. The image at its source is of corpses being consumed without end — abhorrence, not chambers. What Jesus makes of the image beyond its source is exactly the contested question, and this page does not settle it; the three live positions are set out in the scholarship section below, and the site holds the question open on the record.8

A text-critical note on the undying worm

In the KJV, the worm-and-fire refrain appears three times in Mark 9 (vv. 44, 46, 48). In the earliest manuscripts it appears once, at verse 48; verses 44 and 46 are absent from the oldest witnesses and are judged by modern critical editions to be early copyists' amplifications, which is why modern translations skip those verse numbers. Nothing doctrinal hangs on the difference — verse 48 says what the two echoes said — but a reader comparing Bibles deserves to know why the verses "disappear," and a study built on these words owes the disclosure. (Confirmed against the standard critical editions, 2026-07-15: NA28/UBS omit vv. 44 and 46; the worm-and-fire refrain is text-critically original only at v. 48.)

Part four · The prisons that hold no humans

Tartaros, the abyss, and the king named Destruction

Two more terms complete the "hell" vocabulary, and neither contains a single human being.

Tartaros appears in the New Testament only as a verb: God, sparing not the angels that sinned, cast them into Tartaros (ταρταρώσας | tartarōsas | Gk - G5020), committing them "to chains of deepest darkness to be kept until the judgment" (2 Pet 2:4). The verb form is unique to this verse in the New Testament, but the name was not foreign to Greek-speaking Judaism — the Septuagint had already used Tartaros in its own renderings (in Job, and in Proverbs' description of the depths), and Jewish writers like Josephus use it freely.9 Peter borrows the deepest dungeon Greek had and assigns it a population of exactly one kind: angels who sinned, in chains, awaiting judgment. Not the human dead.

The abyss — bottomless pit (ἄβυσσος | abyssos | Gk - G12) — is the working prison of demonic powers in the present age. The demons called Legion beg Jesus "not to order them to go back into the abyss" (Luke 8:31); Revelation's fifth trumpet opens its shaft with a key, releasing smoke and the locust-army (Rev 9:1–2); Satan is bound in it for the thousand years, sealed, released, and finally thrown elsewhere (Rev 20:1–3, 10). Like Tartaros it is intermediate — a cell, not a sentence.

And over the opened abyss, Revelation places a figure whose name completes a trail this study began back in the Hebrew poetry:

"They have as king over them the angel of the bottomless pit; his name in Hebrew is Abaddon, and in Greek he is called Apollyon."

Revelation 9:11 · NRSVue

Abaddon — the word that sat beside Sheol in Job and Proverbs as poetry's personified Destruction, the one that "has no covering" and could "say" things — returns as a proper name with a Greek translation attached (Apollyōn, "Destroyer"). The half-personification of the Wisdom books is a full character in the Apocalypse. Whether John intends a real angelic being, a personification of destruction itself, or a satirical shadow of the destroying empires of his day is argued; what the trail shows either way is a canon whose underworld vocabulary grew — from furniture, to figure, to name.10

Part five · The ends of the map

The lake of fire, paradise, and the resolution by resurrection

The lake of fire is Revelation's term and Revelation's only: the text never calls it Gehenna, Hades, or Sheol. Its sequence is precise. The beast and false prophet enter first (Rev 19:20); the devil follows (20:10) — with "tormented day and night forever and ever" said, in that verse, of those three; then Death and Hades themselves are thrown in (20:14); then "anyone whose name was not found written in the book of life" (20:15). Twice the text supplies its own gloss: "This is the second death" (20:14; 21:8). The lake of fire is what remains when even the realm of death has been disposed of — the one term in this study to which the word final attaches by explicit statement. Whether the "forever and ever" spoken over the devil's trio describes every human consigned there, or whether "second death" means for humans what death ordinarily means — an end — is part of the contested ground held open below.

Paradise is the map's other pole, and the New Testament spends three uses on it: "today you will be with me in Paradise" to the dying thief (Luke 23:43); Paul "caught up into Paradise" where he "heard things that are not to be told" (2 Cor 12:3–4); and the tree of life "in the paradise of God" (Rev 2:7). Paul's expectation for the dead in Christ matches the thief's: "my desire is to depart and be with Christ, for that is far better" (Phil 1:23) — conscious presence, not cold storage, even while the dead are elsewhere called "those who have died" who will "rise first" at the last trumpet (1 Thess 4:13–16). The intermediate state of the righteous, on the New Testament's showing, is with Christ and still waiting — comforted, and incomplete.

Which is the point on which the whole vocabulary finally converges. The Bible's underworld words are not resolved at death; they are resolved at resurrection. Daniel's sleepers wake to two verdicts (Dan 12:2). "All who are in their graves will hear his voice and will come out" to two resurrections (John 5:28–29). Paul plants Hosea's taunt at the moment the trumpet sounds: "Death has been swallowed up in victory. Where, O death, is your victory? Where, O death, is your sting?" (1 Cor 15:54–55). Sheol and Hades are emptied and abolished; Gehenna and the lake of fire lie on the far side of the judgment; paradise gives way to a city where "death will be no more" (Rev 21:4). Every temporary term ends. The permanent ones begin after the verdict — and about the inner nature of the permanent one for the lost, the texts hand us their most vivid imagery and their least settled interpretation in the same breath.

Part six · The scholarship

Where the scholars agree, and where no one should pretend to

Named positions in this section were checked against each scholar's published work (2026-07-15). The four positions set in quotation marks below — Peterson, Stott, Hart, and Wright — are verified direct quotations; the remaining names are represented as fair characterizations of their published views, with the source and page given in the notes.11

Development across the canon

The mainstream account of Sheol is developmental, and the leading monograph complicates the popular picture at its root. Philip Johnston's Shades of Sheol argues that the Hebrew Bible does not present Sheol as the flat, neutral destiny of everyone: the word attaches overwhelmingly to death under judgment or in distress, the righteous are shown descending to it only in extremity — and more often praying to be spared it — and the tidy division of Sheol into compartments for the righteous and the wicked is a Second Temple development that "the Old Testament texts do not assert."15 What genuinely thickens across the canon is the moral differentiation surfacing in the prophets' poetry and the two-directional resurrection stated explicitly only at Daniel 12. John Walton adds the standard caution from genre: early texts' silence about post-mortem differentiation shows what those genres needed to say, not necessarily everything early Israel believed. Between the Testaments the development accelerates on any account — Second Temple literature (1 Enoch's compartments; the Qumran community's eschatology) shows Judaism already holding differentiated, populated pictures of the interim dead before Jesus ever told a story about a rich man and a beggar. N. T. Wright's larger argument frames the whole shelf: what distinguished Jewish hope was never a doctrine of the underworld but the expectation of bodily resurrection out of it — the difference he marks as that between "life after death" and "life after life after death," the latter being resurrection itself.16 Which is exactly the resolution the New Testament texts above assert.

The Gehenna debate — held open, on the record

What Gehenna's fire finally does to those in it is the most contested question in biblical eschatology, and this site's standing rule is that genuinely open questions are surveyed, not settled. The three positions, each with real textual footing:

Eternal conscious torment
The majority tradition of Western Christianity since Augustine. Leans on Rev 20:10's "tormented day and night forever and ever," Matt 25:46's symmetry (the same aiōnios for punishment and life), and the theological argument that guilt against the infinite bears infinite consequence. Robert Peterson reads the very Isaiah image this study traced as pointing past destruction: its "undying worms and inextinguishable fire" point "to the final doom of the wicked — eternal punishment."12
Conditional immortality / annihilation
The wicked are finally destroyed, not endlessly tormented. Leans on Matt 10:28's apollymi ("destroy both soul and body"), Isaiah 66:24's source-image of corpses, and "second death" read as death. Argued at length in Edward Fudge's The Fire That Consumes; John Stott advanced it only tentatively, and candidly: "I find the concept [of eternal conscious torment] intolerable and do not understand how people can live with it without either cauterising their feelings or cracking under the strain."13
Universal reconciliation
All are ultimately restored. Leans on the cosmic-scope texts (1 Cor 15:28; Phil 2:10–11; Col 1:20) and reads aiōnios as "of the age" rather than endless. Von Balthasar held it only as a hope; David Bentley Hart presses it as a claim — that "the universalist understanding of [Christianity's] message is the only one possible," said "without the least hesitation or qualification."14 Its standing objection is Matt 25:46's symmetry.

The honest summary of the field is that each position can cite verses the others must work to accommodate, and that the choice among them turns on judgment calls — how much weight Revelation's imagery can carry, what aiōnios quantifies, whether Isaiah's corpses set the meaning of Jesus' fire — that the texts themselves do not adjudicate. This page's summary table above deliberately marks Gehenna and the lake of fire "final" without specifying the experiential content of that finality. That is as far as the method goes.

The descent — "he went and made proclamation to the spirits in prison"

First Peter 3:18–20 (with Eph 4:8–10 alongside) stands behind the creed's "he descended." Four readings persist: the patristic descent-to-Hades with proclamation to the dead; the reading that takes the "descent" as the incarnation itself; the view that Christ preached through Noah, in Noah's day, by the Spirit; and William Dalton's — now widely followed in critical commentary — that the risen Christ proclaimed victory, not an offer, to the imprisoned angelic spirits of the Genesis 6 rebellion. It is no accident that the leading reading routes through the same 2 Peter 2:4 / Jude 6 territory as Tartaros above: the prison passages hang together, and they are about powers, not people.

Cited but not yet confirmed — the Enochic backdrop (Heiser)

Michael Heiser's The Unseen Realm argues a connected arc behind several of this study's texts: Genesis 6's "sons of God" as a real divine rebellion; 1 Enoch's Watchers narrative as the Second Temple elaboration both 2 Peter 2:4 and Jude 6 allude to; and Revelation 9's opened abyss — with Abaddon/Apollyon over it — as that same imprisoned-powers tradition unsealed. This characterization is recalled from a prior reading and has not been independently checked against the book's pages; no chapter citation is given, and none of this study's claims rests on it. It is shown — flagged as unconfirmed — because it is the best-known popular synthesis of these prison texts, and a reader deserves to know both that the synthesis exists and that this page has not verified it line-by-line. The site's standing caution also applies: the Divine Council framework is engaged seriously here, neither adopted wholesale nor dismissed.

Where this lands

"Hell" is an English filing decision, not a biblical word. Behind it: a realm that holds all the dead and is destined to be emptied (Sheol/Hades, with the pit and Abaddon as its poetry); a burning valley Jesus made the figure of final judgment on the far side of resurrection (Gehenna); prisons for rebel powers that hold no human beings (Tartaros, the abyss); a final state Revelation names only as itself (the lake of fire, the second death); and a waiting comfort the dying Christ could promise a thief before either of them was raised (paradise).

Sorted, the terms produce one sequence the texts state outright — death, the interim, resurrection, judgment, the permanent — and one question they leave contested: what the permanent holds for the lost. The sorting is this study's claim. The contested question stays contested here, by the site's own rule.

Criticism

The strongest objections, left standing

The tidy sequence is a construction. No biblical author lays out "Sheol → resurrection → Gehenna → lake of fire" as a single scheme; this study assembled it from Genesis to Revelation across a thousand years of texts, multiple genres, and at least two languages. Harmonizing Job's silent Sheol, Luke's furnished Hades, and Revelation's emptied one into stages of a single system may credit the canon with an architecture only the systematizer supplied. A reader who thinks Revelation's imagery was never meant as cosmological sequencing — that "Death and Hades thrown into the fire" is a poet's way of saying death dies, not a disposal schedule — loses nothing from the individual word studies and owes this page's overall map no allegiance.

Word-counting is not theology. The method here leans on occurrence tallies and lexical sorting — sixty-five Sheols, twelve Gehennas, one Tartaros. But meaning does not distribute evenly across occurrences, and a term's theology can be carried by texts that never use it (the parable of the weeds burns what it burns without naming Gehenna; the outer darkness has no entry in this study's table at all). The vocabulary-first frame corrects a real error — the KJV's collapse — but it risks the inverse error: treating the map of words as the map of the thing.

The development story leans on contested dating. "Sheol was neutral early and differentiated late" presupposes the standard critical ordering of the texts — Job and the early narratives before the prophetic taunts, Daniel last. Push Daniel earlier, or read the taunt-songs as older tradition, and the clean developmental arc blurs. The site's own practice of naming seams applies to this one: the arc is the scholarly consensus reading, and it is a reading of texts whose dates are themselves argued.

Conclusion

Reading the words before the doctrine

The English word "hell" has been asked to do the work of at least four originals, and the originals refuse to be one thing. Sorting them does not decide the hardest question — it locates it. The debate that matters is not whether Sheol equals Gehenna (it does not; that much is vocabulary), but what the final judgment's fire is and does — and there the texts hand every party imagery and deny every party a diagram.

Where the author lands, held as confession: I take the sequence the sorted words yield — interim, resurrection, judgment, permanence — as the Bible's own frame, and I decline to fill in the permanence beyond what is written, because the writers themselves declined. What I take from the study personally is the direction of the whole vocabulary's movement: every term for death's custody is temporary, every lock has a keyholder, and the only voice in the New Testament that holds "the keys of Death and of Hades" spent three days on the wrong side of the door to get them. The map's contested edges are real, and they stay open on this site. Its center — that death's realm is emptied by resurrection — is not where the ambiguity lives.
