## The three terms

The trace tracks three "characters" and asks where any **two of the three** stand in the same scene:

1. **Z — the sons of Zeruiah:** Joab, Abishai, Asahel (2 Sam 2:18). David's nephews (1 Chr 2:16). Named almost always by the mother.
2. **L — God / the LORD:** counted two ways below, because the difference matters — **invoked** (named in a character's speech, an oath, a wish) vs. **actor** (the narrator reports God *doing* something: a deep sleep, a frustrated counsel, a plague sent or stayed).
3. **D — David.**

The reason to separate "invoked" from "actor": the LORD is named constantly in David's mouth (oaths, blessings, restraint-formulas), but the LORD *acts* in the narrator's own voice in only a handful of these scenes — and those few are exactly the ones Lee flagged.

## The three flagged scenes (verbatim + Hebrew)

### A. 1 Samuel 26 — Abishai, David, and the deep sleep from the LORD

> **26:7** So David and Abishai came to the people by night: and, behold, Saul lay sleeping within the trench, and his spear stuck in the ground at his bolster: but Abner and the people lay round about him.
> **26:8** Then said Abishai to David, God hath delivered thine enemy into thine hand this day: now therefore let me smite him, I pray thee, with the spear even to the earth at once, and I will not smite him the second time.
> **26:9** And David said to Abishai, **Destroy him not**: for who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD'S anointed, and be guiltless?
> **26:10** David said furthermore, As the LORD liveth, **the LORD shall smite him**; or his day shall come to die; or he shall descend into battle, and perish.
> **26:11** The LORD forbid that I should stretch forth mine hand against the LORD'S anointed…
> **26:12** So David took the spear and the cruse of water from Saul's bolster; and they gat them away, and no man saw it, nor knew it, neither awaked: for they were all asleep; **because a deep sleep from the LORD was fallen upon them**.
> **26:23** …for the LORD delivered thee into my hand to day, but I would not stretch forth mine hand against the LORD'S anointed.

**Hebrew (verified, BibleHub interlinear):**
- "a deep sleep from the LORD" = **תַּרְדֵּמַת יְהוָה** *tardēmat YHWH* — *tardēmâh* (**H8639**, "deep sleep, lethargy, trance," from *rādam*). The same God-sent stupor that falls on Adam (Gen 2:21) and Abram (Gen 15:12). It is the LORD's act that makes the infiltration possible; Abishai reads it as delivery and wants to strike.
- "Destroy him not" = **אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ** *al-tašḥîtēhû* — Hiphil of **שָׁחַת** *šāḥat* (**H7843**, "to destroy, ruin"). **Hold this verb** — it is the same root that names the destroying angel in scene C.
- "delivered" (26:8) = *sāgar* (**H5462**, "shut up, surrender, deliver up") — the agency idiom here is *sāgar*, not *nātan* (corpus note, `forfeited_access` review 2026-06-09; not re-verified this session).
- "I will not smite him the second time" (26:8) — echoes verbatim in Joab/Amasa: "struck him not again" (2 Sam 20:10). The brothers' single clean stroke is a recurring tic.

**The shape:** the LORD acts (sleep); the son of Zeruiah wants to be the striking hand ("let me smite"); David refuses the role and assigns the striking to God alone ("the LORD shall smite him"). David will not let Z be the instrument against the anointed.

### B. 2 Samuel 17–18 — the LORD defeats the counsel, then Joab strikes

> **17:14** And Absalom and all the men of Israel said, The counsel of Hushai the Archite is better than the counsel of Ahithophel. **For the LORD had appointed to defeat the good counsel of Ahithophel, to the intent that the LORD might bring evil upon Absalom.**
> **17:23** And when Ahithophel saw that his counsel was not followed… he… hanged himself, and died…
> **18:5** And the king commanded Joab and Abishai and Ittai, saying, Deal gently for my sake with the young man, even with Absalom…
> **18:9** …and his head caught hold of the oak, and he was taken up between the heaven and the earth…
> **18:14** Then said Joab, I may not tarry thus with thee. And he took three darts in his hand, and thrust them through the heart of Absalom, while he was yet alive in the midst of the oak.
> **18:16** And Joab blew the trumpet, and the people returned from pursuing after Israel: for Joab held back the people.

**Hebrew (verified):**
- "had appointed" = **צִוָּה** *ṣiwwāh* (**H6680**, Piel, "commanded, ordained, purposed").
- "to defeat" = **לְהָפֵר** *ləhāpēr* — Hiphil of **פָּרַר** *pārar* (**H6565**, "to break, frustrate, annul, make void").
- "might bring evil" = **הָרָעָה** *hārāʿāh* (**H7451**, "evil, calamity, disaster") — **the same word that returns in scene C** ("the LORD repented him of *the evil*").

**The shape:** the LORD is the offstage actor who *appoints* Absalom's ruin (frustrating the counsel that would have killed David that night, 17:1–4); Joab is the onstage hand who *executes* it, against David's explicit order. The divine purpose and the son of Zeruiah's darts land on the same body. David is the father who cannot restrain either.

### C. 1 Chronicles 21 / 2 Samuel 24 — God sends the destruction; the LORD stays it

> **2 Sam 24:1** And again the anger of the LORD was kindled against Israel, and he moved David against them to say, Go, number Israel and Judah.
> **24:3** And Joab said unto the king… **why doth my lord the king delight in this thing?**
> **24:15** So the LORD sent a pestilence upon Israel… and there died of the people from Dan even to Beersheba seventy thousand men.
> **24:16** And when the angel stretched out his hand upon Jerusalem **to destroy** it, **the LORD repented him of the evil**, and said to the angel that destroyed the people, **It is enough: stay now thine hand.** And the angel of the LORD was by the threshingplace of Araunah the Jebusite.
> **1 Chr 21:1** And **Satan** stood up against Israel, and provoked David to number Israel.
> **1 Chr 21:6** But Levi and Benjamin counted he not among them: for the king's word was abominable to Joab.
> **1 Chr 21:15** And God sent an angel unto Jerusalem **to destroy** it: and as he was destroying, the LORD beheld, and **he repented him of the evil**, and said to the angel that destroyed, **It is enough, stay now thine hand.**
> **1 Chr 21:27** And the LORD commanded the angel; and he put up his sword again into the sheath thereof.

**Hebrew (verified, BibleHub interlinear on 2 Sam 24:16):**
- "to destroy" = **לְשַׁחֲתָהּ / הַמַּשְׁחִית** *ləšaḥătāh / hammašḥît* — **שָׁחַת** *šāḥat* (**H7843**). **The same verb David spoke to Abishai in scene A** ("destroy [*šāḥat*] him not"). In 1 Sam 26 a son of Zeruiah is told *not* to *šāḥat* the LORD's anointed; in the census the LORD halts His own *mašḥît* (destroyer).
- "repented him" = **וַיִּנָּחֶם** *wayyinnāḥem* — Niphal of **נָחַם** *nāḥam* (**H5162**, "relent, be sorry, console oneself") — the relenting verb the site's good/evil work already turns on.
- "of the evil" = **הָרָעָה** *hārāʿāh* (**H7451**) — same word as 2 Sam 17:14.
- "stay now thine hand" = **הֶרֶף יָדֶךָ** *heref yādekā* — Hiphil imperative of **רָפָה** *rāphāh* (**H7503**, "slacken, let drop, stay"). The hand is stayed.
- "Satan" (1 Chr 21:1) = **שָׂטָן** *śāṭān* (**H7854**) — the same noun David throws at the sons of Zeruiah in 2 Sam 19:22 ("ye should this day be **adversaries** unto me," *ləśāṭān*).

**The shape:** here the son of Zeruiah (Joab) plays *David's* role from scene A — the one who **objects** to the act, calls it abominable, and will not fully carry it out (leaves Levi and Benjamin uncounted). David plays the role of the one who *insists* on the wrong thing. And the LORD is both the striking hand and the staying hand: He sends the *mašḥît* and then says *heref* — relenting (*nāḥam*) of *the evil* (*hārāʿāh*).

|--------|-------------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **šāḥat** H7843 "destroy" | David: "**destroy** him not" (to Abishai) | — | the angel "**to destroy**"; the LORD halts "the **destroyer**" |
| **rāʿāh** H7451 "evil/calamity" | — | LORD "bring **evil** upon Absalom" | LORD "repented him of the **evil**" |
| **nāḥam** H5162 "relent" | — | — | LORD "**repented**" |

| **śāṭān** H7854 "adversary" | — | (cf. 2 Sam 19:22, David → Z) | "**Satan** provoked David" |
| "smite not the second time" | Abishai 26:8 | — | (cf. Joab/Amasa 20:10) |

The cleanest single observation: **šāḥat** ("destroy") is spoken by David *to* a son of Zeruiah in A, and is what the LORD *halts* in C. The verb of restraint that David lays on Abishai is the verb of divine restraint at the threshing floor.

## The structural hypothesis (Lee's) — and where it breaks

**The reading the data supports:** across these scenes there is a three-body system — a striking hand (the son of Zeruiah), a restraining/judging will (alternately David or the LORD), and the king at the center. The "instrument of violence" role and the "restraint" role are **not fixed to a person**; they rotate:

- **A (1 Sam 26):** D restrains Z; **L** reserved as the only legitimate striker ("the LORD shall smite him").
- **B (2 Sam 17–18):** **L** ordains; Z executes; D cannot restrain.
- **C (1 Chr 21):** Z restrains/objects; D insists; **L** both strikes and stays Himself.

Read that way, the son of Zeruiah is the human figure who *does the thing the principal will not do with his own hand* — and the LORD is the one figure who occupies **both** ends of the system (He delivers Saul yet forbids the strike through David's mouth; He ordains Absalom's death and executes it through Joab; He sends the plague and stays it Himself). The sons of Zeruiah are, on this reading, the visible human echo of a divine prerogative the text keeps reserving to God: the right to strike, and the right to stay the hand.

**Where it holds:** the three "actor-L" scenes line up unusually well, and the *šāḥat* / *rāphāh* / *nāḥam* / *rāʿāh* vocabulary genuinely recurs and clusters (verified, not impressionistic). The Joab Study's own "Abishai surfaces violence / Joab acts / David between" reading is a subset of this, and this adds the divine axis the Study only gestures at.

**Where it breaks (state these before leaning on it):**
1. **"Two of three" is too loose to be evidence by itself.** By the weak test (any two named), the pattern is nearly every scene Joab is in — which means it predicts almost nothing. The *strong* test (L as narrator's actor) is what isolates the three; the framing should be stated that way or it over-claims.
2. **The sons of Zeruiah are not one role.** Abishai *proposes* the violence (1 Sam 26; 2 Sam 16; 19); Joab *commits* it (Abner, Uriah, Absalom, Amasa); Asahel *receives* it (2 Sam 2). Collapsing three brothers into one "instrument" smooths over a distinction the narrator keeps (the Study already flags this as "not airtight" — Abishai is named with Joab at Abner's killing, 3:30, and heard the Absalom order without striking).
3. **The role-rotation is a reader's symmetry, not a stated one.** The text never says David in A and Joab in C are doing "the same thing." The chiasm (D-restrains-Z / Z-restrains-D) is real and pleasing but is supplied by the reader; that is the move the Marginalia's own boxes warn about ("a reading that has an answer for everything has usually supplied a few of its own").
4. **Word-frequency is unchecked.** *šāḥat*, *rāʿāh*, *nāḥam*, *rāphāh* are common verbs; their co-occurrence here may be ordinary narration, not a signature. This needs the same frequency check the *ḥōmeš* note got before any weight rests on it — and the local OSHB index does not yet cover Samuel (only Haggai), so the lemma counts would have to come from BibleHub/BLB.

**Net:** the strong form of the pattern (the three actor-L scenes, bound by *šāḥat*/*rāphāh*/*nāḥam*/*rāʿāh*, with the strike/stay prerogative reserved to God) is worth taking seriously and is textually grounded. The weak form ("two of three keep appearing together") proves little and should be retired as a load-bearing claim. The role-rotation chiasm is a hypothesis, attractive but reader-supplied — flag it SPECULATIVE.

## Addendum (2026-06-27): The instrument — *shebet*, arms availability, and the "why three?" problem

*Added at Lee's request, following the "dart" word-study. Two questions braided: (a) what weapons a Judahite actually had in this period, and (b) what Joab's "three *shevatim*" (2 Sam 18:14) physically were. BDB entry for H7626 verified firsthand (BibleHub); all Hebrew verified vs. interlinear. KJV throughout.*

### The arms backdrop — scarcity, then recovery

The period opens under a Philistine stranglehold on metalworking. **1 Sam 13:19–22:** *"there was no smith found throughout all the land of Israel: for the Philistines said, Lest the Hebrews make them swords or spears… So… there was neither sword nor spear found in the hand of any of the people that were with Saul and Jonathan."* Two cautions the text itself enforces: it says **no smith** (*ḥārāsh*), not "no iron" — the popular "iron monopoly" gloss is an inference, and modern scholarship doubts a specifically *iron* secret (early iron was not yet superior to good bronze; the control was over **smithing in general**). Verse 21's sharpening-fee, a **pim** (an otherwise-unknown word the KJV could only guess as "a file"), was vindicated when inscribed *pym* weight-stones turned up at Gezer, Ekron, Ashdod, Timnah — a real Iron-Age price-unit, ≈⅔ shekel.

So under Saul the Judahite militia fought with what a farmer/shepherd owned: mattock, axe, ox-goad, **sling** (*qelaʿ*, the Benjaminite specialty, Judg 20:16; David, 1 Sam 17:40,50), **bow** (*qeshet*; "teach Judah the bow," 2 Sam 1:18), and the **staff/club** (*shebet*, *maqqel*). Sword (*ḥereb*) and spear (*ḥanit*) were scarce, prestige items — David flees with none (1 Sam 21:8) and is handed **Goliath's own sword**, kept as a relic behind the ephod (21:9). David then reverses the shortage: his band girds swords (1 Sam 25:13, largely captured Philistine kit), he breaks Philistine power (2 Sam 5; 8), and by the end his mighty men are spearmen (Adino vs. 800, Abishai vs. 300, 2 Sam 23:8,18). **By the Absalom war, David's army is well-armed.** That matters for what follows.

### The weapon-word: *shebet* as the underdog's implement

BDB (H7626) lists, under sense 1.a "rod, staff, club," a specific military use: *shebet* **"as (inferior) weapon (opposed to *ḥanit*) 2 Sam 23:21 (= 1 Chr 11:23); compare *maqqel* 1 Sam 17:40."** That is precisely the Benaiah scene — verified: *"the Egyptian had a spear (*ḥanit*) in his hand; but he went down to him with a staff (*baššāḇeṭ*, H7626)."* So the lexicon itself classes the *shebet* as the **lesser** weapon a man brings against a spearman — the same word, and the same underdog posture, as David's *maqqel* (shepherd's staff) against Goliath. The *shebet* is the weapon of the man who hasn't got a proper one.

### Lee's "why three?" — the case for a disposable shaft

Lee's objection to my earlier "club" gloss: *you do not need **three** clubs.* Three of something, driven into a hanging man's heart and left there, points to **expendable, single-use, pointed shafts** — darts, light javelins, sharpened stakes — not a reusable bludgeon. The syntax backs him:

- **Held in the palm.** *bᵉḵappô* (H3709, the hollow/palm of the hand) — he gripped all three *in one hand*. You can palm three darts or short stakes; you cannot palm three war-clubs or three full spears.
- **The verb is "drive in," not "throw."** *wayyitqāʿēm* (*tāqaʿ*, H8628, "thrust, drive, drive in"). So these were **hand-driven into** the body at close range, not hurled — consistent with short, pointed, expendable shafts staked into a helpless target.
- **The lexicon already concedes the sense.** BDB gives 2 Sam 18:14 its **own** sub-entry, 1.b: *shebet* = **"shaft, i.e. spear, dart."** It is the word's *only* occurrence in that sense — the context forces it. Strong's gloss likewise: "a stick (for punishing, writing, **fighting**, ruling, walking)"; KJV-usage includes "**dart**."
- **The ancient versions agree.** The **LXX read βέλη** ("darts / missiles"), and a line of critical scholars (Thenius, Wellhausen, Budde, Hertzberg, H. P. Smith) emend the Hebrew to **שְׁלָחִים *shᵉlāḥîm*** (H7973) — the ordinary word for a **thrown weapon / missile / dart** (KJV "darts" at 2 Chr 32:5; Joel 2:8). So the oldest translator *and* the modern text-critics independently landed where Lee's instinct points: pointed, throw-or-drive, disposable.

**Net: Lee is right, and this corrects my "blunt club" reading.** The best-supported picture is three light, pointed, expendable shafts — darts / short stakes / light javelins — palmed and driven in by hand; "three" reflects their single-use character. KJV "darts," the LXX βέλη, and the *shᵉlāḥîm* emendation are all already reaching for exactly this.

### Counterweights (so the reading is held honestly)

1. **"Three" might be emphatic, not literal disposability.** Hebrew narrative uses small numbers for force/brutality, and v.15 immediately piles on — *"ten young men… compassed about and smote Absalom."* Overkill is the scene's signature; "three then ten" may simply mean *made very sure*, in which case "three" implies thoroughness, not single-use shafts. (This is a real alternative; it does not defeat Lee's reading, but it means "three ⇒ disposable" is an inference, not a proof.)
2. **The word's default sense is rod/staff/club.** 18:14 is the lone "dart/shaft" instance in 190 occurrences — so "dart" here is **contextual**, leaning on syntax and the versions, not on the word's normal meaning.
3. **Tension with the scepter/tribe wordplay.** The disposable-weapon reading and the earlier *shebet* = "scepter/tribe/rod" irony pull against each other: the harder you press "these were specialized darts," the more you invite the **emendation to *shᵉlāḥîm***, which erases the *shebet* pun entirely. You can keep the pun **or** maximize the dart-specificity, not both at full strength. *Lectio difficilior* slightly favors keeping the MT *shevatim* (a scribe would more likely "fix" odd "rods" into normal "missiles" than the reverse) — which preserves both the wordplay *and* an "ad-hoc pointed shaft" sense, the most economical landing.

### Bearing on Joab

Because David's army was **not** disarmed by the Absalom war, Joab's reach for *shevatim* over a sword is a **choice**, not a poverty of arms — which sharpens rather than softens the ugliness: a commander who could carry any blade finishes a defenseless, hanging man with three expendable shafts driven in by hand, then lets ten armor-bearers finish the work. The *shebet*'s native register — the **underdog's** lesser weapon (Benaiah, David's *maqqel*) — is here inverted: the strong man uses the weak man's implement on a helpless target. Whether the narrator intends that inversion or it is a reader's resonance is, as ever, better marked than asserted.

*To check next: confirm the Thenius/Wellhausen/Budde/Hertzberg/Smith emendation against their primary texts (rule 22 — BDB's sigla are not the sources themselves); pull the LXX (Rahlfs / Brooke-McLean) at 2 Sam 18:14 to confirm βέλη and whether it reflects *shᵉlāḥîm*; check whether *tāqaʿ* + a thrown weapon is attested elsewhere (it usually governs driving-in, e.g. tent-pegs, Judg 4:21).*

## Addendum 2 (2026-06-27): The wound-words — *ḥōmesh* (belly) vs. *lev* (Absalom)

*Verified: BDB H2570, H3820 (BibleHub); interlinear 2 Sam 18:14, 20:10.*

**The four stealth-kills land in the belly.** The wound-word in the Asahel/Abner/Ish-bosheth/Amasa cluster is **חֹמֶשׁ *ḥōmesh* (H2570)** — KJV "the fifth rib," but the lexica (BDB, Strong's) read it flatly as **belly / abdomen**. The "rib" is a translator's guess (hearing *ḥōmesh* ≈ *ḥāmēsh*, "five," and supplying the italic *rib*). The narrative excludes the chest reading outright: at 2 Sam 20:10 the *ḥōmesh* blow spills Amasa's **bowels** (*mēʿeh*, H4578) "to the ground" — intestines are abdominal, not thoracic; you cannot disembowel a man with a heart/lung strike. So *ḥōmesh* = the soft underbelly below the ribcage. (A deep/upward thrust there can still reach the great vessels and upper-abdominal organs tucked under the lower ribs — hence instant death with no second blow — but those are abdominal, not the heart/lungs.) **The published Marginalia footnote has been updated to cite 20:10 as the clincher.**

**Absalom is the exception — and "pierced through the heart" needs a caveat.** 2 Sam 18:14: Joab thrust the three *shevatim* *bᵉlev Avshalom*, KJV "through the **heart** of Absalom." Two cautions:

1. **The word is *lev* (H3820), which means heart *or* midst/middle.** The very same verse uses *bᵉlev* again three words later — *"in the **midst** (*bᵉlev*) of the oak."* So "the *lev* of Absalom" can be read "the **midst/chest/body** of Absalom," not necessarily the anatomical heart. Most translations render "heart," but the word does not force the cardiac organ.
2. **The narrative undercuts a clean heart-shot.** After the three blows Absalom is *"yet alive in the midst of the oak"* (18:14), and **ten armor-bearers then "compassed about and smote Absalom, and slew him"** (18:15). A pierced heart is instant death; this was not — it took ten more men to finish him. So whatever the three *shevatim* struck, it was not an immediately-fatal heart strike.

**Net:** "pierced through the heart" is the standard rendering and is defensible, but the better-supported picture is a thrust into the **chest/torso** (*lev* = the midst of him) that wounded without instantly killing — consistent with expendable shafts (Addendum 1) and with the gang-finish of v.15. The narrator's anatomy is deliberate: the four treacherous stealth-kills go in the **belly** (*ḥōmesh*); Absalom alone is taken in the **chest/heart** (*lev*) — in open battle, by a swarm, not by a single quiet blade in a gate.
