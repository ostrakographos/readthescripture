# Critical Study: Abner, Ner, H5216 & H5369
**Why Are the Two "Ner" Names Different?**

## 2. H5216 — נֵר (nēr): "Lamp / Light / Candle"

### Hebrew Form

The consonantal root is **נר** (nun-resh). With Masoretic pointing, the most common form is **נֵר** (*nēr*, tsere under the nun). Strong's lists several orthographic variants:
- נֵר (*nēr*) — most common form
- נִיר (*nîr*) — lengthened form
- נֵיר (*nêyr*) — variant
- נֵרָה (*nērâh*) — feminine form

### Etymology

BDB traces H5216 to a primitive root (related to H5214 and H5135 — the Aramaic/Hebrew root for "fire, to burn, to shine") meaning **"to glisten" or "to shine."** The lamp is thus the "gleaming thing" — the object that gives off light. This root underlies both the Aramaic נוּר (*nûr*, H5135, "fire") and the Hebrew usage.

HALOT (Hebrew and Aramaic Lexicon of the Old Testament) defines נֵר as a masculine noun: **"lamp, light"** — specifically the oil-burning lamp with a wick, the standard domestic and cultic light-source in the ancient Near East.

### Usage in the Hebrew Bible

The word covers a range of literal and figurative uses:

- **Cultic/Tabernacle lamp:** "And thou shalt make the seven lamps thereof" (Exod 25:37, KJV) — the menorah's individual flame-holders
- **Temple lamps:** "And the candlesticks of pure gold" (1 Kgs 7:49, KJV) — נֵר in the Temple of Solomon
- **Household lamp:** "Her candle goeth not out by night" (Prov 31:18, KJV)
- **Search metaphor:** "I will search Jerusalem with candles" (Zeph 1:12, KJV)
- **Joy/merrymaking:** "the light of the candle shall shine no more at all in thee" (Jer 25:10, KJV)
- **Figurative — divine word:** "Thy word is a lamp unto my feet" (Ps 119:105, KJV)
- **Figurative — dynastic continuity:** "Yet the LORD would not destroy Judah for David his servant's sake, as he promised him to give him alway a light" (2 Kgs 8:19, KJV) — נֵר used as a metaphor for the perpetuation of David's line
- **Figurative — Abner himself:** "Thou shalt go no more out with us to battle, that thou quench not the light of Israel" (2 Sam 21:17, KJV) — applied to David

> **Note:** The KJV uses "candle" in most cases where NRSV/modern versions prefer "lamp" — this reflects the domestic context of the era of translation (1611), when candles were the household norm. The Hebrew נֵר refers specifically to an oil lamp, not a wax candle. The NRSVue consistently renders it "lamp."

## 3. H5369 — נֵר (Nēr): The Personal Name

### Hebrew Form

The Hebrew is **identical** to H5216 in both consonantal form and Masoretic pointing: **נֵר** (nun-resh, with tsere). There is no orthographic or phonetic distinction whatsoever between H5216 and H5369 in the Hebrew text. They are the same word.

### BDB and HALOT Treatment

BDB (Brown-Driver-Briggs) lists H5369 simply as: **"= נֵר q.v."** — that is, it is the *same word* as H5216, used here as a proper name, meaning "lamp." BDB does not assign H5369 an independent etymology; it derives it wholly from H5216.

HALOT similarly treats Ner (H5369) as a **hypocoristic personal name** derived from the common noun *nēr* ("lamp"), without any separate root analysis.

### Why Did Strong's Assign a Separate Number?

James Strong (in *Strong's Exhaustive Concordance*, 1890) consistently assigned different index numbers to **common nouns** and the **proper names derived from them**, even when the Hebrew spelling was identical. This was a pragmatic indexing decision, not a linguistic one. His intent was to help English readers quickly distinguish between:

- H5216 found in Psalm 119:105 ("a lamp unto my feet") — *nēr* as a noun
- H5369 found in 1 Samuel 14:51 ("Ner the father of Abner") — *Nēr* as a personal name

The underlying Hebrew word is the same. **H5369 is not a different Hebrew word from H5216 — it is the same word functioning as a proper name.** Both BDB and HALOT confirm this. Strong's number separation is a classification device, not a claim of linguistic difference.

This pattern is common across the Hebrew Bible: Rachel (רָחֵל, "ewe"), Deborah (דְּבוֹרָה, "bee"), Tamar (תָּמָר, "palm"), and Ner all represent the practice of using common nouns as personal names. The person named "Ner" was named "Lamp."

### Who Is Ner (H5369)?

According to the biblical record, Ner was a Benjaminite — the father of Abner, commander of Saul's army (1 Sam 14:50). The genealogy of 1 Samuel 14:50–51 states:

> **"And Kish was the father of Saul; and Ner the father of Abner was the son of Abiel."** (KJV, 1 Sam 14:51)

Hebrew: וְקִישׁ אֲבִי־שָׁאוּל וְנֵר אֲבִי־אַבְנֵר בֶּן־אֲבִיאֵל

This places both Kish (Saul's father) and Ner (Abner's father) as sons of Abiel, making Saul and Abner **first cousins**.

## 4. H74 — אַבְנֵר (ʾaḇnēr): Abner

### Hebrew Form and Variant

H74 has two forms in the Hebrew text:
- **אַבְנֵר** (ʾaḇnēr) — short form, by far the most common
- **אֲבִינֵר** (ʾăḇînēr) — longer form with medial yod (י), appearing rarely; likely the more original or formal form

### Etymology

H74 is a **compound name**, identified by Strong's and confirmed by BDB as derived from:

- **H1 — אָב** (ʾāḇ) = "father"
- **H5216 — נֵר** (nēr) = "lamp"

The full meaning is **"my father is a lamp"** or more idiomatically **"father of light / enlightening."** The yod in the variant form אֲבִינֵר (ʾăḇînēr) is a pronominal suffix — it is literally "my father is Ner/lamp" (אֲבִי = "my father"). The short form אַבְנֵר is a contraction, as is common in Hebrew names (compare אֲחִינֹעַם / Ahinoam → contracted forms appear elsewhere).

This means Abner's very name contains the word *nēr* (H5216). His name encodes the same noun that became his father's proper name. The naming is almost certainly intentional: **Ner named his son "My father is a lamp,"** embedding *nēr* into his son's name as a tribute to himself or as a family identity marker.

## 5. The Genealogical Problem: Two Accounts, One Family

The most critical textual issue surrounding Ner (H5369) is the apparent contradiction between the Samuel narrative and the Chronicles genealogy.

### Account A — 1 Samuel 14:50–51 (KJV)

> *"And the name of the captain of his host was Abner, the son of Ner, Saul's uncle. And Kish was the father of Saul; and Ner the father of Abner was the son of Abiel."*

Hebrew of v. 51: **וְקִישׁ אֲבִי־שָׁאוּל וְנֵר אֲבִי־אַבְנֵר בֶּן־אֲבִיאֵל**

**Structure implied:**
```
Abiel
├── Kish → Saul
└── Ner → Abner
         (cousins)
```

**Note on v. 50b:** The KJV says Abner was "Saul's uncle" — but the Hebrew (דּוֹד שָׁאוּל) could mean "uncle of Saul" or "kinsman of Saul." If v. 51 is correct (Ner and Kish are brothers), then Abner is Saul's **cousin**, not uncle. The KJV translation "uncle" is debated; some scholars read it as applying to Ner (Ner was Saul's uncle), with Abner being the cousin. This ambiguity in v. 50 has generated considerable discussion.

### Account B — 1 Chronicles 8:33 and 9:39 (KJV)

> *"And Ner begat Kish, and Kish begat Saul..."* (1 Chr 8:33, repeated verbatim at 9:39)

**Structure implied:**
```
Ner
└── Kish → Saul
(and separately, Ner → Abner)
```

Under this reading, Ner is Saul's **grandfather** — placing Abner and Saul in an uncle-nephew relationship, not as cousins.

### Scholarly Positions

Three main explanations have been offered:

**Position 1 — Two different men named Ner (most widely held)**
There was a grandfather named Ner (Chronicles) and a separate son or descendant also named Ner (Samuel) who was Abner's father. The grandfather Ner fathered Kish (Saul's father), and the second Ner — possibly named after the grandfather — fathered Abner. Naming sons after grandfathers was standard Hebrew practice (cf. the "papponomy" pattern seen in the Elephantine papyri and later Second Temple texts). This is probably the most defensible resolution, as it requires no emendation of either text and fits known Israelite naming customs.

**Position 2 — Abiel and Ner are the same person**
Some have proposed that "Abiel" (אֲבִיאֵל, "God is my father") was Ner's second name or title, and that 1 Samuel is referencing the same ancestor as Chronicles. This would compress the discrepancy: the man known as Ner in Chronicles is the same as Abiel in Samuel. However, this solution creates its own tensions, since 1 Sam 9:1 identifies Kish as "the son of Abiel" without any indication Abiel = Ner.

**Position 3 — Skipped generations**
The genealogy in Chronicles may telescope generations, skipping one or more intermediate ancestors. Ancient genealogies were not always sequentially complete (cf. Matthew's genealogy of Christ which deliberately omits generations). Under this view, "Ner begat Kish" may mean "Ner was an ancestor of Kish" with intervening names omitted.

**Assessment:** Position 1 (two men named Ner) is the most textually economical solution. It accounts for both texts without emendation, aligns with Hebrew naming customs, and avoids the forced identifications required by Position 2. *This is my own preferred assessment in agreement with most commentators, though certainty is not possible given the available evidence.*

## 6. Summary: Why Are the Two "Ner" Names Different?

The short answer: **they are not linguistically different.** H5216 and H5369 are the *same Hebrew word* (נֵר, *nēr*), spelled identically, pointed identically, and pronounced identically. James Strong assigned them different concordance numbers in 1890 purely for indexing purposes — to distinguish the **common noun** ("a lamp") from its use as a **proper name** ("Ner the person"). Every major lexicon (BDB, HALOT, Gesenius) confirms this: H5369 is derived from and identical to H5216.

The person named Ner (H5369) was literally named **"Lamp."** His son's name, Abner (H74), incorporates the same word: **"My father is a lamp."** This is a deliberate, generationally linked naming pattern. The family carried the metaphor of light in their very names.
