# Exhibits — workbench pieces on display

*Everything else in this repository is the finished record: the site as served, the standards it is held to, the grades and corrections. This directory is different. These are pieces of the workbench itself — working documents shown as specimens, the way a museum displays a craftsman's bench alongside the finished cabinet.*

## What an exhibit is, and is not

An exhibit is a working document, selected and put on display. It is **not** a site claim. Specifically:

- **Ungraded and unreviewed.** Exhibits have not passed the blind grading, verification, or editorial passes that published pages pass. Some contain errors — in at least one case, errors the project knows about and has deliberately left in place (see the piece's card below).
- **Frozen.** An exhibit is a snapshot as of its stated date. The workbench copy may have moved on; the exhibit doesn't.
- **Never evidence.** No published Study may cite an exhibit as support for anything. (The same rule the site applies to its labeled-opinion pieces.)
- **Positions in motion.** A working note records where thinking was that day, not what the project asserts. The site's pages — not these documents — carry its considered claims.

## How pieces are selected

Selection is the editor's judgment, against four tests:

1. **It shows the trade honestly** — the reading, checking, arguing, and correcting that precede a page, including the parts that didn't work.
2. **It stands alone well enough to read** — a visitor without the workbench can follow it.
3. **Nothing private or third-party rides along** — no personal material, no other people's content, no unverified claims about living persons presented as settled.
4. **Its value is as a specimen, not a claim** — a piece goes on display for what it shows about the process, not to smuggle an ungraded argument into the public record.

## The exactness rule

The default is an **exact copy** of the workbench file — byte-for-byte, imperfections included. Where something cannot cross the privacy or third-party line — personal material, other people's content, quarantined personal files — it is **redacted on the record**: a visible marker in the text, never a silent rewrite. (This is a deliberate exception to this repository's usual rule that public documents are authored fresh rather than redacted — for an exhibit, the artifact itself is the object, so a marked redaction is the honest tool. A silently edited exhibit would be a forgery of a workbench document.) Each card below states whether its piece is exact or carries redactions.

One thing is deliberately **not** redacted: the names of internal workbench files — trackers, standards documents, working notes — that exhibits refer to in passing. The editor's ruling (2026-07-14): the names are harmless and several are mentioned on the site itself; what stays private is those files' content, not their existence. (An earlier display of one exhibit masked four such names; the marks were removed under this ruling — the repository's history shows both states.)

## An invitation

Exhibits are auditable by design. Run the site's own rubric against one (see [`GRADING.md`](../GRADING.md)), check its citations, or compare it with what was eventually published — the card says whether a published counterpart exists. Finding a defect in an exhibit is the exhibit working as intended.

## The pieces

### `abner-ner-critical-study.md` — Abner, Ner, and two Strong's numbers *(2026-06-27)*

**What it is.** A workbench study chasing a narrow question: why does Strong's concordance assign two different numbers (H5216, H5369) to what looks like the same Hebrew word, *nēr* — "lamp" — once as a noun and once as Abner's father's name? The chase runs through the lexica, the KJV's translation choices, the name-etymology of Abner ("my father is a lamp"), and lands in a genuinely disputed genealogical contradiction between 1 Samuel 14 and 1 Chronicles 8 — three scholarly resolutions weighed, one preferred, certainty declined.

**Why it's on display.** It is a complete small specimen of the workbench: a question most readers would never think to ask, pursued until it turned into a real text-critical problem, with the reasoning and sources visible the whole way. It also shows what workbench output looks like *before* the editorial passes — which is the point of the next paragraph.

**Condition note — read before trusting anything in it.** This piece is shown **exact and uncorrected**, and the imperfections are part of the exhibit. Defects found in an internal review, deliberately left in place in the piece and noted here on the card instead:

- §2's usage list has an entry labeled "Figurative — Abner himself" whose quoted verse (2 Sam 21:17, "quench not the light of Israel") is said of **David** — the label contradicts its own content.
- The H5216 KJV-mapping table counts **Matt 5:15** under "candle" — a Hebrew Strong's number cannot occur in the Greek New Testament; the "(parallel)" hedge does not belong in a count column.
- The "HALOT defines / HALOT treats…" attributions are backed by nothing in the source list (which is entirely Blue Letter Bible / BibleHub / StudyBible pages) — secondhand lexicon claims the site's publication standard would have required verifying firsthand.

The list may not be exhaustive — the reader is invited to keep going: grade the piece against the site's rubric, check its claims, or simply read it more suspiciously than its confident tone invites. There is **no published counterpart** — this piece never became a page, so the exhibit is also an example of workbench output that didn't ship.

**Exactness:** exact copy, no redactions.

### `man_of_god_1kings13_method_2026-06-28.md` — the workbench behind a published page *(2026-06-28)*

**What it is.** The working notes behind the site's 1 Kings 13 pieces — the session in which the chapter (the man of God from Judah, the lying prophet of Bethel, the lion on the road) was read, fact-checked, argued over, and shaped toward publication. The `[Lee]` / `[Claude]` tags are the workbench convention for keeping the editor's directives and readings distinct from the model's inferences; they are shown as-is because the division of labor is part of what the document records.

**Why it's on display.** This is the one exhibit with **published counterparts**: the method piece [*A Hard Chapter in the Open*](https://readthescripture.com/a_hard_chapter_in_the_open.html) and the Study [*The True Word in a False Mouth*](https://readthescripture.com/the_true_word_in_a_false_mouth.html) both grew out of these notes, so a visitor can hold the workbench in one hand and the shipped pages in the other. And the notes happen to record the method working under load, in both directions: a fact-check catching errors (Bethel, not Dan; the Saul's-bones parallel tested and discarded — kept deliberately, as a specimen of a curiosity let go); the editor's grammatical reading of verse 3 sent to a blinded adversarial check and **ruled an over-read** — while the same check part-vindicated the underlying instinct through a mechanism neither party had proposed; and the citation rule firing on an unverified commentary block, which the notes refuse to lean on. That last thread has a visible ending: the unverified attributions flagged in §2 were later reconciled on the published method piece, where the unconfirmed material is set apart in its own labeled block — an open item in the workbench, closed on the page.

**Condition note.** Ungraded working notes, dated 2026-06-28; the published pages, not these notes, carry the site's considered claims. The theological engagement in §5 is explicitly marked held-not-settled in the text itself.

**Exactness:** exact copy, no redactions. (Its first display carried four filename redactions; removed under the exactness rule's naming ruling above.)

### `receipt_1kings13_study_publish_2026-06-28.md` — the receipt that shipped the Study *(2026-06-28)*

**What it is.** Every working session that touches content ends with a written record — a *receipt*. This is the one that shipped [*The True Word in a False Mouth*](https://readthescripture.com/the_true_word_in_a_false_mouth.html): an authoritative state table (what published, at what grade, with which gates still open), the durable items promoted out of the session, the open-gates delta, and a narrative log in which every load-bearing claim is tagged `[Lee]` or `[Claude]` so the next reader knows whose judgment it was.

**Why it's on display.** With this piece the gallery holds a complete provenance chain for one work: the workbench notes (the exhibit above) → this receipt → the grade log → the published pages. The receipt's claims are checkable against the rest of this repository: the three blind runs it records appear in [`grading/grades.jsonl`](../grading/grades.jsonl) (runs 1–3, dated 2026-06-28), and the verse-by-verse NRSVue verification list can be held against the live page. It also shows the record's habit of deflating its own good news — the grade is reported with its limit attached: *"3 same-model runs cluster at A+ 20/20, which is the optimistic ceiling, not the real gate."* And the log preserves a pushback running the uncomfortable direction: the model correcting the editor's framing of a discovery, on the record, in the session that shipped the piece.

**Condition note.** The receipt names internal workbench files (trackers, standards, a working note) that are not part of this repository — the names stand per the exactness rule above. It predates the letter-grade retirement (2026-07-14), so it records a letter grade the site's pages no longer display; the numeric scores are unchanged. The wiring note at the end preserves a period detail: the session was fighting a file-corrupting sandbox mount, and says so.

**Exactness:** exact copy, no redactions.

### `zeruiah_lord_david_triad_2026-06-27.md` — a hypothesis under test *(2026-06-27)*

**What it is.** A workbench trace of a structural hypothesis: three "characters" — the sons of Zeruiah (Joab, Abishai, Asahel), the LORD, and David — tracked across every scene in Samuel–Kings–Chronicles where two of the three stand together. A seventeen-row co-occurrence table; three scenes read closely with the Hebrew verified word by word; a table of the lexical threads that bind them (*šāḥat*, *rāʿāh*, *nāḥam*, *rāphāh*, *śāṭān*); and two addenda on the physical realia — what Joab's three *shevatim* actually were, and where the narrator puts each wound.

**Why it's on display.** This is the method at the **hypothesis stage** — earlier than anything else in the gallery. The document's own "where it breaks" section lists four ways the pattern might be the reader's construction rather than the text's, *before* any of it is allowed near a published page; its addenda record the collaboration correcting in the uncomfortable direction — the editor's objection ("you do not need *three* clubs") overturning the model's earlier reading, with the lexicon, the syntax, and the ancient versions cited on the way down. The published Joab pages ([Study](https://readthescripture.com/joab_indispensable_adversary.html), [Marginalia](https://readthescripture.com/joab_from_gibeon_to_the_altar.html)) hold a reading this document calls a subset of the hypothesis; the hypothesis itself never shipped.

**Condition note.** Marked **SPECULATIVE** in its own header, and that label is load-bearing: the structural "triad" reading is a hypothesis under test, not a site claim — the verse-and-Hebrew data is verified, the pattern built on it is not settled. A list of text-critical scholars (Thenius, Wellhausen, Budde, Hertzberg, Smith) is flagged *inside the document* as cited from lexicon sigla, unconfirmed against their primary texts. There is no published counterpart for the central hypothesis.

**Exactness:** exact copy except **one redaction** — the name of a personal file under standing quarantine, the one class of name the exactness rule still masks.

### `sons_of_zeruiah_draft.html` + `sons_of_zeruiah_draft_intact_2026-06-15.html` — the study that refused to conclude *(2026-06-13)*

**What it is.** A complete Study draft on the three sons of Zeruiah — Asahel the runner, Abishai the volunteer, Joab the commander — reading each brother through three lenses (scholar, skeptic, commentary tradition) and deliberately declining to reconcile them. Its stated method is the refusal: the residue these brothers leave — two deaths narrated and one withheld, no prophetic or divine word ever addressed to any of them — is precisely what no single lens closes, and forcing a synthesis would manufacture an answer the text withholds.

**Why it's on display.** The Samuel material held more stories than the site had pages for; what shipped from that season is live — the backbone timeline ([Judges → Kings](https://readthescripture.com/judges_to_kings_timeline.html)), the Joab Study ([*The Indispensable Adversary*](https://readthescripture.com/joab_indispensable_adversary.html)), the David/Saul study ([*After God's Own Heart*](https://readthescripture.com/david_saul_character_study.html)), and later a narrative telling of Joab's life ([Marginalia](https://readthescripture.com/joab_from_gibeon_to_the_altar.html)). This draft is a road not taken. A cold blind read judged its openness earned rather than evasive, its material is real, and it was set aside anyway: the working record's stated reason is overlap — too many pieces circling one family as the work refocused onto one figure — and the editor's retrospective is plainer: it didn't land, and the author, not necessarily the study, needed a narrower object. The intuition it carries — that the brothers matter structurally to David's story — did not die with it; it returns, weeks later and after publication, in the triad document elsewhere in this gallery, chased in tables instead of lenses, and withheld again. What the live site may yet do with any of this, the gallery doesn't know.

**Condition note — two copies, one artifact.** This exhibit is shown twice, and the pair is deliberate. The copy under the workbench filename is the file as the workbench holds it today: **truncated**, ending mid-citation at "Bodner," — 1,350 bytes short, missing the tail of the source list, the author block, and the closing markup. The damage was environmental: the era's sandbox mount corrupted file operations (the same issue the receipt exhibit's closing note records as a period detail). The same session caught that corruption on three *published* pages and repaired them; this file was already in the reject pile, so its damage went unnoticed until it was selected for display (2026-07-15). The second copy, `sons_of_zeruiah_draft_intact_2026-06-15.html`, is the same file as committed to the workbench's history on 2026-06-15, complete to its closing tag — recovered so the visitor can read what the tail lost. The truncated copy is byte-identical to the first 57,486 bytes of the intact one; nothing differs but the missing end. Both are shown so nothing is hidden: the artifact as history actually left it, and the full text beside it. The environmental issue has since been resolved; the scar is kept because it is part of the record.

Beyond the damage, read the draft's own flags before trusting it: each commentary-lens card ends *"Attributions here are drawn from memory and flagged for verification before publication"* — that verification never ran; footnote 1 flags the Valley-of-Salt numbers crux (2 Sam 8:13 / 1 Chr 18:12 / Psalm 60) for firsthand checking that never happened; footnote 5 concedes the Hebrew name glosses were never confirmed against a critical lexicon. The on-page A+ 19/20 is a same-model workbench grade that appears nowhere in this repository's grade log, and its own caveat lists the gates that never closed. The editor's name appears in the body ("Lee's parallel," "the thing Lee saw") — the workbench convention of recording whose observation each point was, left intact.

**Exactness:** two exact copies, no redactions — the current workbench file (truncation included), and its 2026-06-15 committed state recovered from the workbench's history.

### `the_sword_and_the_silence.html` — published for one day *(2026-06-13)*

**What it is.** The same-day sibling of the Zeruiah study above: the same brothers threaded into a single narrative with a controlling figure — a family named only for its mother, a sword that builds the kingdom of man and is refused at the altar of God, a God who uses these men without ever speaking to them. It reaches back to Simeon and Levi for a type, sideways to Assyria for the category "instrument," and lands on the divine silence as the residue.

**Why it's on display.** This piece was drafted, revised, blind-graded, published to the live site — navigation entry, homepage card — and rejected by the editor before the day ended. This copy is the only one that survives. Two things are worth the visit. First, the gap between the grade and the gate: it was blind-graded A+ 20/20 twice, once as a draft and again after revision, and pulled anyway — the rubric can measure whether an argument is honest, consistent, and scoped; it cannot measure whether a controlling figure commits the site to decoding a silence the text leaves mute. Second, the piece knew its own risk and said so everywhere: the perspective callout concedes *"that throughline is a reading, not something the text asserts"*; the typology section carries its own strongest objection (the Simeon–Levi pattern *"is a pattern the reader assembles"*); a conflict box demotes the piece's most quotable move (*"bless and curse are one word"* — two different Hebrew words; the pun is not operating here). Every joint was fenced honestly, and the whole was still the wrong kind of thing for the site's method: the direction on rejection was to drop the synthesized telling and go fact-forward at the characters — the road that became the published Joab Study. The site has since added a labeled-opinion category (Marginalia), and its published Joab piece tells this same life as a story; whether the readings shelved here would fare differently under that label is not something this gallery knows.

**Condition note.** The on-page A+ 20/20 is a same-model workbench grade, absent from this repository's grade log; its own caption concedes the cold grade does not close the contestable typology. The open items it shipped with never closed: footnote 7's roster of blood-shedders who meet a divine word is *"illustrative, not a proof by exhaustion,"* and the exhaustive sweep it defers never ran; the commentator positions in the source list (Alter, McCarter, Brueggemann, Halpern, Baden, Bodner) were never confirmed against the primary scholarship; one scripture block is deliberately KJV (Isa 10:5, noted on-page) against the site's NRSVue default — the piece's own grade caption claims "two KJV citations," a miscount left uncorrected in the frozen copy. The Hebron city-of-refuge point is framed on-page as the reader's irony, not the text's stated charge. The honesty devices worked; keep them in view while reading.

**Exactness:** exact copy, no redactions.
