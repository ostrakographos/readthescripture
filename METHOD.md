# Method and Editorial Standards

*What every page on readthescripture.com is held to, and why. The site's pages describe the reading method for readers; this document states it as the standards the work is checked against.*

## The reading method

1. **Read the text first, naively.** Form a working view of what the passage says before consulting commentaries, study notes, or doctrinal frameworks. Frameworks are consulted after, to challenge the working view — not before, to supply it.
2. **Separate the text from the tradition.** What a text says and what a tradition says it must mean are different claims and are labeled as such. Neither is dismissed; they are kept distinguishable.
3. **Apply the same scrutiny to the site's own positions.** A standard of evidence used against a view the site disputes is applied, identically, to the views the site holds. A method that only audits opponents is not a method.
4. **Name what is unknown.** Where the text, the languages, the manuscripts, or the history leave a question genuinely open, the page says so and leaves it open. Resolving an open question by assertion is treated as an error, not a style choice.

## Verification standards

These are checked before publication, not assumed:

- **Quoted Scripture is verified against the named edition.** A verse quoted with a version label (NRSVue, KJV, etc.) is checked against that version's actual text — not reconstructed from memory, which demonstrably blends similar translations. The site's primary translation is the NRSVue; where a rendering is itself the contested point, multiple translations are shown side by side.
- **Original-language claims are verified against the lexicon.** Statements about Hebrew or Greek words — semantic range, occurrence counts, parsing — are checked against standard references (Strong's, BDB, primary-text concordance searches), and occurrence claims are sampled against the text itself. An English word-search is not evidence about a Hebrew word.
- **Naming a scholar's position is a citation.** Attributing a view to a named scholar — even as context rather than proof — is a falsifiable claim about what that scholar wrote, and is confirmed against their own text before it ships. A search-engine summary is not a source.
- **Sources carry position labels.** Every source cited in a Study is labeled with its theological or academic position (e.g., conservative evangelical, mainline Protestant, Catholic, Orthodox, historical-critical, secular/skeptical). Labels are not softened to make a source more comfortable to cite.
- **Every page stands alone.** Each page makes its own case from primary and external evidence. Site pages do not cite each other as authority; a reader who never sees a second page must get the complete argument on the first.

## Voice standards

- **Superlatives must be earned or absent.** "Never," "always," and "the most X in all of Scripture" are removed unless the text actually supports them.
- **The opposing reading gets stated with real force, once.** Counter-readings live in a dedicated objections section, stated as their proponents would state them — not pre-rebutted throughout the body, and not strawmanned.
- **Open questions stay open.** Genuinely disputed theological questions are not quietly settled by a page's framing. Where the page's author holds a position, the position is named as one, in its place.
- **No self-narration.** A page shows restraint and honesty by its structure; it does not spend prose telling the reader how restrained and honest it is being.

## Content types

- **Study** — long-form exegesis or argument. States its bias where load-bearing, cites and position-labels sources, and carries a grade block (see `GRADING.md`).
- **Resource** — a visual reference guide (timelines, maps, summaries). Accurate and plain; carries no argument and no grade.
- **Marginalia** — labeled opinion. Names its lean up front and carries an honesty checklist instead of a grade. A Study may never cite a Marginalia piece as evidence.

## Corrections

Errors on published pages are fixed on the page and logged in `CORRECTIONS.md`: what was wrong, what is right, and what settled it. A correction inherits the full verification standard — the entire citation is re-derived from the primary record, not just the disputed part.

## AI collaboration, disclosed

Prose on published pages is composed by Claude (Anthropic) in collaboration with, and under the editorial control of, the site's author and editor, Lee Sadler. Every page carries the byline. Two consequences are treated as standing risks and engineered against rather than denied:

1. **Fluent error.** A language model produces confident, plausible prose whether or not the underlying claim is verified. The verification standards above exist precisely because the drafting process cannot be trusted to catch its own fabrications.
2. **Agreeable drift.** A model tends toward its collaborator's framing over time. The grading procedure (fresh instances that did not write the piece, plus periodic different-lab models — see `GRADING.md`) exists to put readings in front of judges that do not share the working context.
